What is TIG?

TIG measures how systems hold together using a 10-operator algebra with dual composition tables. It produces falsifiable predictions. Everything is open.

TIG stands for Trinity Infinity Geometry — but the name is less important than what it does. Take any signal (text, audio, sensor data). Map it to a 5-dimensional force vector. Compute the second derivative (curvature). Classify that curvature into one of 10 operators. Compose those operators through a fixed table. Measure how often the result is HARMONY. That fraction is the system's coherence. That's TIG.

The entire mathematical core fits in about 1 KB: a 26-entry force lookup table, a 3-stage shift register, a 10×10 composition table, and a 32-sample coherence window. No neural networks. No training data. No weights. Just fixed-point arithmetic on a composition algebra.

For Skeptics

Here are the 9 ways to kill TIG. Every claim has a kill condition. If you can trigger one, we want to know.

1
CL Table is Trivial

If random 10×10 tables with the same boundary constraints routinely produce 73% HARMONY, the table is not special. Test: Monte Carlo over 10 million random tables.

2
D2 Classification is Noise

If structured text and random bytes produce identical operator distributions, the pipeline captures nothing meaningful. Test: Chi-squared + t-test on 1,000 real texts vs. 1,000 random strings.

3
T* = 5/7 is Arbitrary

If a parameter sweep finds a better threshold (more than 0.03 away from 5/7), the value is not a natural phase boundary. Test: Sweep 0.50 to 0.90 on 500 labeled truth claims, measure F1.

4
Wave Scheduling Wastes Energy

If TIG wave scheduling uses more power than constant scheduling on identical workloads, the adiabatic claim fails. Test: A/B power measurement over 10,000 compute cycles.

5
BTQ is No Better Than Random

If the BTQ decision kernel selects outcomes no better than uniform random, the least-action scoring is cosmetic. Test: 10,000 decision episodes, compare mean coherence delta.

6
Hebrew Encoding is Arbitrary

If semantically related inputs produce no more similar glyph patterns than unrelated inputs, the D2 encoding captures no structure. Test: Cosine similarity on 500 semantic pairs vs. 500 random pairs.

7
Scale Breaks Determinism

If the same input produces different operator sequences at different clock speeds, the fixed-point math is not actually fixed. Test: Compare Python 50Hz vs. FPGA 200MHz on 10,000 identical inputs.

8
Gravity Doesn't Help Learning

If gravitational topic weighting produces no better coherence growth than uniform random selection, information gravity is cosmetic. Test: 30-day A/B on identical topic pools.

9
Wobble Adds Only Noise

If Tesla/Einstein wobble physics reduces coherence without improving exploration diversity, the perturbation is harmful, not helpful. Test: Shannon entropy + coherence delta with alpha=0 vs. alpha>0.
