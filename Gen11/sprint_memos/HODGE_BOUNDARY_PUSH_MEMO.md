## PART 2 — Best Boundary-Push Testbed

**"The best boundary-push testbed is a simple principally polarized abelian 4-fold of Weil type — specifically one with $\mathrm{End}^0(A) \supseteq K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$ for some squarefree $d$ — because the Hodge structure is completely explicit (determined by the period matrix and the endomorphism algebra $K$), the cohomology decomposes into $K$-eigenspaces that are individually computable, the algebraic subspace of $H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}$ is known to be generated by the $K$-invariant part (from endomorphism-induced correspondences), and the cokernel reduces to exactly the $K$-anti-invariant Weil classes — a specific, finite-dimensional, explicitly constructible rational subspace whose algebraicity is the first genuinely hard case."**

Why not other families:
- **Products of surfaces**: Künneth decomposition resolves most of $H^{2,2}$ algebraically (cross-terms from $H^{1,1}(S_1) \otimes H^{1,1}(S_2)$); the remaining problem reduces to the Hodge conjecture for individual factors, not a new codimension-2 phenomenon
- **Complete intersections**: the Lefschetz hyperplane theorem extends the shell far into $H^{2,2}$; the obstruction starts at higher codimension
- **Hyperkähler 4-folds (K3-type)**: special deformation structure constrains $H^{2,2}$ strongly; many results known but the structure is quite different from generic 4-folds
- **Generic non-CM 4-folds**: the endomorphism algebra is trivial ($\mathrm{End}^0 = \mathbb{Q}$), so there are NO extra algebraic cycles beyond $L^2$; the cokernel is maximally large and unstructured, with no handle on it

Weil 4-folds are the Goldilocks case: enough extra structure (the $K$-action) to decompose and target the cokernel, but not so much structure (like full CM) that the problem trivializes.

## PART 3 — Observable Side for Weil 4-Folds (Exact Checklist)

For a principally polarized abelian 4-fold $A$ with $\mathrm{End}^0(A) \supseteq K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$:

| Observable quantity | Computable? | What it gives |
|--------------------|-----------|-|
| Hodge decomposition $H^{p,q}(A)$ | YES — from period matrix $\Omega \in M_{4\times 4}(\mathbb{C})$ | Complete structure of all $H^{p,q}$ |
| $K$-eigenspace decomposition of $H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}$ | YES — from the $K$-action on $H^1(A)$ | Splits $H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}$ into $K$-invariant and $K$-anti-invariant parts |
| Image of $\mathrm{cl}^2$ on known cycles | YES — compute class of $L^2$, and classes of graph correspondences from elements of $\mathrm{End}(A)$ | Identifies the algebraic subspace of $H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}$ |
| The Weil class $w \in H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}(A,\mathbb{Q})^{K\text{-anti}}$ | YES — from explicit formula in terms of period matrix and the $K$-action | The specific target class whose algebraicity is the open question |
| $\dim\,\mathrm{coker}(\mathrm{cl}^2\vert_\mathrm{prim})$ | YES for specific $A$ — count algebraic vs total rational $(2,2)$ classes | Measures the "size" of the surviving obstruction |
| Symmetries and automorphisms of $A$ | YES — from the Albert algebra of $\mathrm{End}^0(A)$ | Extra algebraic cycles from automorphism-induced correspondences |
| Néron-Severi group $\mathrm{NS}(A) = H^{1,1}(A,\mathbb{Z})$ | YES — from period matrix arithmetic | All divisors, Chern classes, Lefschetz classes |

**What is NOT observable:**
- Whether the Weil class $w$ is algebraic (this is exactly the open problem)
- Any algebraic cycle whose class would land in the $K$-anti-invariant part (none is known for generic Weil 4-folds)

## PART 4 — What "Shrinking the Cokernel" Means

**"A real boundary push in Hodge would mean proving that for a specific Weil 4-fold $A$ of Weil type $(K, L)$, the cokernel of $\mathrm{cl}^2\vert_\mathrm{prim}$ is strictly smaller than the full $K$-anti-invariant subspace of $H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}(A,\mathbb{Q})$ — specifically, that the Weil class $w$ is in the image of $\mathrm{cl}^2$, realized by an explicit algebraic cycle $Z \in \mathrm{CH}^2(A)_\mathbb{Q}$ with $\mathrm{cl}^2(Z) = w$, thereby shrinking the cokernel from dimension 1 to dimension 0 for that specific variety."**

Concrete benchmarks:
1. **Minimal progress:** show $\dim\,\mathrm{coker}(\mathrm{cl}^2\vert_\mathrm{prim}) < \dim H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}(A,\mathbb{Q})^{K\text{-anti}}$ for ANY specific Weil 4-fold
2. **Gap 2 closure:** show $\mathrm{coker} = 0$ for a SPECIFIC Weil 4-fold with prescribed period matrix
3. **Full Gap 2:** show $\mathrm{coker} = 0$ for ALL Weil 4-folds of a given type

## PART 5 — Best Tactic: Endomorphism Algebra Decomposition

**The tactic:** decompose $H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}(A, \mathbb{Q})$ under the action of $\mathrm{End}^0(A) = K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$ into eigenspaces, and try to construct algebraic cycles for each $K$-eigenspace separately using:
1. Graph correspondences $\Gamma_\phi \in \mathrm{CH}^4(A \times A)$ from endomorphisms $\phi \in \mathrm{End}(A)$, then restrict/push forward to get cycles in $\mathrm{CH}^2(A)$
2. The Pontryagin product on the Chow ring $\mathrm{CH}^*(A)$
3. The Künneth decomposition if $A$ is isogenous to a product $A_1 \times A_2$

**Why this is strongest:**
- The $K$-decomposition is computable (explicit from the period matrix)
- Algebraic cycles from endomorphisms are constructive — they can be written down explicitly
- For isogenous products: the Künneth decomposition reduces the problem to cycles on lower-dimensional factors where more is known
- The $K$-invariant part is ALREADY in the image of $\mathrm{cl}^2$ (generated by endomorphism correspondences); only the $K$-anti-invariant Weil class remains

**Why NOT cycle construction in general:** for a GENERIC abelian 4-fold (no endomorphisms), there are no natural "extra" algebraic cycles to try; the endomorphism algebra approach requires $\mathrm{End}^0(A) \supsetneq \mathbb{Q}$, which is why Weil 4-folds are the right testbed.

## PART 6 — Next Concrete Hodge Object

**"The next concrete Hodge object is the Weil class $w_A \in H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}(A,\mathbb{Q})$ for a specific principally polarized abelian 4-fold $A$ of Weil type $(K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d}), L)$ with prescribed period matrix — the unique (up to scaling) rational primitive $(2,2)$ class in the $K$-anti-invariant subspace of $H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}(A,\mathbb{Q})$ — and the question: does there exist a codimension-2 algebraic cycle $Z \in \mathrm{CH}^2(A)_\mathbb{Q}$ with $\mathrm{cl}^2(Z) = w_A$?"**

Concrete computation plan:
1. Fix $d = 1$ (Weil 4-fold with $K = \mathbb{Q}(i)$) and a specific simple principally polarized $A$
2. Compute $H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}(A, \mathbb{Q})$ explicitly from the period matrix
3. Decompose under the $\mathbb{Q}(i)$-action into $+i$ and $-i$ eigenspaces
4. The $-i$ eigenspace contains $w_A$ — write it down explicitly
5. Compute all known algebraic classes (from $L$, from endomorphisms, from sub-abelian varieties)
6. Check: is $w_A$ in their span?

This is a finite linear algebra computation once $A$ is fixed.

## PART 7 — Comparison to BSD

**The analogy:**

- **BSD:** build the missing joint object — the Stark-Heegner generator of $E^{77}(\mathbb{Q})$ with height $\det(H)$. The object is predicted to EXIST (the L-function encodes its height), and the task is CANONICAL CONSTRUCTION from analytic data.
- **Hodge:** build the missing primitive cycle — an algebraic cycle $Z \in \mathrm{CH}^2(A_\mathrm{Weil})$ with $\mathrm{cl}^2(Z) = w_A$. The object is PREDICTED to exist (the Hodge conjecture says it should), and the task is explicit ALGEBRAIC CONSTRUCTION from the Hodge data.

**The exact difference:**

In BSD, the object whose construction is sought (the Stark-Heegner point) is provably PRESENT in $E(\mathbb{Q})$ — we have P₁, P₂ explicitly; the question is whether the L-function can produce them canonically. The arithmetic object EXISTS; only the canonical formula is missing.

In Hodge, we do NOT know whether the required algebraic cycle exists. The Weil class $w_A$ is a Hodge class (of type (2,2)), so it satisfies a necessary condition for algebraicity. But the Hodge conjecture says it SHOULD be algebraic; we have no proof of existence and no candidate cycle. The problem is therefore HARDER: not "can the analytic side construct a known arithmetic object?" but "does the required algebraic object even exist?"

This is the deepest structural difference between BSD and Hodge despite their formal similarity: BSD's construction failure is about ACCESS to a known object; Hodge's construction failure is about EXISTENCE of the object itself.

## PART 8 — Strongest Honest Claim

**"Among the five branches, Hodge is the one where the observable side may still be most directly extendable by explicit construction on special families: the Weil 4-fold testbed has a computable period matrix, an explicit $K$-eigenspace decomposition, and a well-defined target class $w_A$ that is a finite linear algebra computation away from being either constructed (if some known cycle happens to represent it) or confirmed as a genuine obstruction (if no linear combination of known cycles reaches it)."**

## PART 9 — Strongest Honest Boundary

**"What is not yet established is whether shrinking the primitive cokernel on Weil 4-folds — by constructing $w_A$ as an algebraic cycle for specific prescribed period matrices — teaches anything stable about the generic codimension-2 Hodge problem, or only about the special Weil structure: specifically, the $K$-eigenspace decomposition is available only because $\mathrm{End}^0(A) \supset K$, and for a generic abelian 4-fold with $\mathrm{End}^0(A) = \mathbb{Q}$, the same decomposition does not exist and the entire $H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}$ is potentially unalgebraic with no structural handle."**

## Collaborator Paragraph

The Hodge boundary push targets the Weil 4-fold as the most computationally accessible hard case. For a simple principally polarized abelian 4-fold $A$ with $\mathrm{End}^0(A) \supseteq K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$ (Weil type), the Hodge structure decomposes completely under the $K$-action: $H^{2,2}_\mathrm{prim}(A,\mathbb{Q})$ splits into $K$-invariant and $K$-anti-invariant parts. The algebraic cycles from endomorphism correspondences generate the $K$-invariant part (this is computable and known); the $K$-anti-invariant part contains the Weil class $w_A$ — a specific rational primitive (2,2) class whose algebraicity is the first genuinely hard case. The next concrete Hodge object is $w_A$ itself: write it down explicitly from the period matrix, compare it to the span of all known algebraic classes (those from $L^2$, from endomorphisms, from sub-abelian varieties), and determine whether $w_A$ is in that span. This is a finite linear algebra computation for any specific $A$ with prescribed period matrix. If yes: $\mathrm{coker}(\mathrm{cl}^2\vert_\mathrm{prim}) = 0$ for that variety — a genuine boundary push. If no: we have found the first explicit verification that the cokernel is non-zero for that $A$ (evidence against Hodge, not a counterexample — the Hodge conjecture might still hold if another cycle construction works). The comparison to BSD is instructive: BSD's construction failure is about accessing a known rational point; Hodge's is about the existence of the algebraic cycle itself, making it the harder construction problem in the spine.
