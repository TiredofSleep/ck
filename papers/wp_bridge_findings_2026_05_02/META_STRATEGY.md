**Purpose:** Strategic notes from the meta-computation done in the

the alignment moves available.

## §1 The four buckets of meta-framework attempts

Historical pattern from research:

**Bucket 1 — Landed and changed how fields connect.**
Examples: Atiyah-Singer index theorem (1963), Grothendieck schemes
(1960s), category theory (Eilenberg-Mac Lane 1945, then Lawvere),
univalent foundations / homotopy type theory (Voevodsky, 2010s),
applied category theory community (2018→present, ACT 2026 in Tallinn
will be the 9th annual conference).

Common features:
- Started narrow and earned breadth incrementally
- Produced specific, citable, technical results domain experts could
  engage with using their own machinery
- Authors did NOT claim breadth that hadn't been demonstrated
- Lawvere proved equivalences (algebraic theories, elementary topoi);
  he did not claim category theory was the substrate of all mathematics

**Bucket 2 — Promising work, partial reception, ongoing.**
Constructor theory (Deutsch-Marletto), twistor theory (Penrose), loop
quantum gravity (Smolin, Rovelli), causal set theory (Sorkin).

Common features:
- Specific technical results in narrow areas
- Authors credentialed within mainstream
- Small but real research communities
- Annual workshops, peer-reviewed venues, PhD students

**Bucket 3 — Big claim, sharp dismissal.**
Wolfram's *A New Kind of Science* (2002), Wolfram Physics Project
(2020), Eric Weinstein's "Geometric Unity," various independent TOEs.

Common features:
- Big claim made before earning the territory
- Self-published or hostile to peer review
- Author posture treats institutional skepticism as obstacle
- Real technical content gets buried under framing

**Bucket 4 — Fringe / crank.**
TOEs by people without training in relevant fields. Numerology, claims
of suppression, conspiracy framing. Don't get engaged with at all.

## §2 Where TIG sits, and what predicts the bucket

The five-finding handoff has Bucket 1 properties. Sharp technical
claims, mechanically verifiable, narrow scope honestly bounded.

The fifteen-ropes document has Bucket 3 risk markers — big claims
across many domains, made before earning the territory, with
sovereign-license framing that reads as preemptive defense against
institutional capture.

**The structural difference between Bucket 1 and Bucket 3 is not the
size of the eventual claim. Grothendieck's claims are vast in
retrospect. The difference is the order of operations.**

Bucket 1 earned the territory one technical result at a time and let
the breadth emerge. Bucket 3 claimed the territory first and tried to
fill it in afterward.

## §3 Concrete alignment moves, ordered by leverage

### Move 1: Engage the Applied Category Theory community

ACT is the community that has built legitimacy for meta-framework work
over the past six years. The 2024 Oxford proceedings span "classical
mechanics, quantum physics, probability theory, linguistics, decision
theory, machine learning, epidemiology, thermodynamics, engineering,
and logic" — exactly the cross-domain territory you're claiming.

ACT 2026 (Tallinn, July 6-10) submissions closed March 2026. Window for
2026 is past. ACT 2027 is the realistic target.

CT 2026 (Baltimore, July 13-18) submissions also closed January 2026.

**Realistic 2026 path:**
- Post paper to arXiv now (math.RA primary, math.CO and math.CT
  secondary)
- Attend ACT 2026 / CT 2026 as observer (CT 2026 registration open
  through May 15, 2026; virtual is free)
- Build engagement over rest of 2026
- Submit to ACT 2027 / CT 2027 in early 2027

### Move 2: Find the analogue of John Baez

Baez is the mathematician most responsible for ACT's existence. His
career is a template for building legitimacy for meta-framework work
through patient cross-domain bridging via the n-Category Café blog,
the Azimuth project, the Adjoint School.

You probably can't get Baez. But there are people structurally similar
in adjacent communities:
- **David Spivak** (Topos Institute, formal applied category theory)
- **Brendan Fong** (Topos Institute co-founder)
- **Bob Coecke** (categorical quantum mechanics, ZX-calculus)
- **Eugenia Cheng** (mathematical popularization)

The Topos Institute (founded 2020, Berkeley) does exactly the kind of
meta-lens work that connects categorical structure to applied domains.

**The move:** identify two or three such people whose work intersects
yours, read enough of their published work that you can engage
technically, send a *short* email (NOT the fifteen-ropes document) with:
- A two-sentence summary of the algebraic object
- The arXiv preprint link
- The verification script
- "I'd value your perspective"

Most won't respond. One might.

### Move 3: Replace defensive disclosure with constructive disclosure

The fifteen-ropes / sovereign-license posture is *defensive* — protecting
against enclosure. The Bucket 1 alternative is *constructive* — write
specific technical results, post them to arXiv (which timestamps and
preserves), present at peer-reviewed venues (which adds credibility),
let breadth emerge from accumulated specifics.

**arXiv is itself a sovereignty mechanism.** Anything posted there is
timestamped, publicly accessible, citable. Posting a paper on the
substrate's algebraic structure to arXiv does the disclosure job that
the sovereign license is trying to do, while also giving you a citable
academic artifact.

The sovereign-license document and the arXiv preprint can coexist, but
they should be doing different jobs:
- arXiv preprint = public claim that earns engagement
- Sovereign document = broader framing for those who go looking after
  engaging with the technical work

### Move 4: One rope, fully proved, before claiming all fifteen

Of the fifteen ropes, the one with the most concrete technical content
right now is the algebraic structure rope — the BHML magma, the role
partition, the trefoil characterization. This is *one rope*. It's the
rope where the verification script actually verifies things. It's the
rope where ACT and category theory communities have machinery to
engage.

**Bucket 1 move:** write a paper on this one rope. Get it reviewed.
Get it cited. Then write the second rope's paper. The fifteen-ropes
document becomes a research program, not a claim.

### Move 5: Calibrate "supposed to" language

You said: "this is supposed to be a meta lens to unite the domains."

Honest version, calibrated to Bucket 1: "I'm developing a framework
that I hope will function as a meta-lens across these domains. The
verified results are the algebraic substrate. The cross-domain
connections are research hypotheses I'm investigating with whoever
wants to engage. The framework hasn't proven its meta-lens function
yet; that would require domain expert engagement to test. I'm staking
the framework publicly so the question can be asked openly across
fields."

That language is defensible to mathematicians. The earlier "TIG IS
so(10) GUT with finite combinatorial substrate" language is not.

The shift is from *assertion* to *hypothesis under investigation*.

### Move 6: Build collaborator base before institution

The institution vision has one member. Bucket 1 path: the institution
is what you have *after* three or four collaborators have come on
board, not what you announce while still being one person.

For TIG: get one collaborator whose lens is genuinely different from
yours — a number theorist who finds the BHML successor interesting, a
category theorist who finds the role magma interesting, a quantum
information person who finds the Cl(8) gate structure interesting. Then
a second. Then a third. Then announce the institution.

The institution vision is sound. The timing should be earned.

## §4 What NOT to do

**Don't** submit the fifteen-ropes document, in current form, to IHÉS
or any mainstream institution. Even with meta-lens framing properly
explained, the verification-tag inflation across speculative claims
will trigger Bucket 3 categorization at first read.

**Don't** lead with sovereign-license framing in any first contact with
mainstream mathematicians. The framing reads as preemptive defense
against enclosure that nobody is attempting yet, which signals a
different epistemic frame than the one mathematicians use.

**Don't** send anything to Friston, Faggin, Tononi, Hoffman, or other
senior figures before having one or two ACT-community engagements.
Senior figures have very limited time and rely on reputation signals to
filter incoming work. A reference from someone in their adjacent
technical community is worth orders of magnitude more than cold
contact, however well-framed.

That sequence — verified facts → peer-reviewed paper → collaborators →
institutional engagement — is what every Bucket 1 framework actually
went through.

The September 11 2026 IHÉS pitch, in its current form, asks IHÉS to do
the work of evaluating a meta-lens framework cold. That's not how IHÉS
engagements typically start. They start with someone IHÉS already knows
saying "you should hear from this person."

**That doesn't mean don't go to France in September.** It means the
trip's purpose shifts from "pitch IHÉS" to "make contact, share the
arXiv preprint, ask thoughtful questions about adjacent work, plant
seeds." The pitch comes later, with referrals.

## §6 The principle to hold

**Don't sand the math down to the size of the verification, and don't
inflate the claims to the size of the ambition. State exactly what you
have. Let the territory grow as the verifications grow.**

The five findings are real. The arXiv paper is built on them. That's
enough for May 2026. More will come, but only as it earns itself.
