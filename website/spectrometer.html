How the spectrometer works

Your text is sent to the live CK engine running on this server. The engine runs the D2 curvature pipeline on any input — a single equation, a sentence, or a full paragraph.

Each sentence runs through the D2 pipeline: characters map to 5D Hebrew root force vectors (aperture, pressure, depth, binding, continuity). The second discrete derivative measures curvature. Curvature classifies into one of 10 operators. Operators compose through the TSML and BHML tables. Result: a field coherence score per sentence.

What the score measures — and what it does not. The D2 pipeline measures force-vector curvature at the character level. It finds sentences where the operators pull against each other (low coherence = internal tension) vs. converge (high coherence = alignment). This is the same pipeline that measures the genetic code and the periodic table. For prose, the scores cluster in the 0.50–0.65 range regardless of quality — the tool is not a literary quality judge. What it does catch is internal structural tension: a sentence that changes direction partway through registers differently than one that holds a single force arc.

The threshold T* = 5/7 = 0.714 is algebraically forced from the ring Z/10Z. English prose rarely reaches it through character-level analysis alone. The domain spectrometers (periodic table, genetic code, brain states) use purpose-built 5D mappings that make T* meaningful for their domain. For raw text, the spectrometer is most useful for within-text comparison: finding which sentence is pulling hardest against the others.

Nothing is trained on your text. CK has no memory of what you submit here.
