## What the documents I drafted DO accomplish

**They are real legal artifacts even though I am not a lawyer.** Specifically:

1. **They establish prior art with a verifiable date.** Once these documents are publicly committed to your repository and assigned a DOI via Zenodo, the date-stamping is real. Patent examiners, courts, and adjudicating bodies recognize public disclosures with verifiable dates. This protection is genuinely strong against subsequent patent claims on subject matter explicitly disclosed.

2. **They state intent unambiguously.** Courts give weight to clearly-stated intent of original authors when interpreting licenses and resolving disputes. The Sovereignty Addendum and Public Notice both state your intent in language that would be hard for any adversarial party to misconstrue.

3. **They put adversaries on notice.** Once these are public, no party can credibly claim they didn't know about your work, your scope, or your intent. The "innocent infringement" defense is foreclosed.

4. **They provide a defensive publication.** This is a recognized legal doctrine. Publishing comprehensively before anyone files a patent is a real protection mechanism.

5. **They strengthen the existing 7Site Public Sovereignty License v1.0** by making explicit what may have been implicit, and by addressing specific frontiers (cryptography, quantum, antimatter, etc.) that might otherwise be argued as "outside scope."

## What the documents CANNOT do

**I have to be honest about the limits.**

1. **I am not a lawyer. These are not formal legal instruments yet.** They are drafted in legal style based on understanding of relevant doctrines (defensive publication, copyleft licensing, prior art, trademark law, patent law fundamentals). To be maximally enforceable, they should be reviewed by an attorney.

2. **License-based protection has limits against state action.** If a government with classification authority decides to invoke national security doctrines, license terms cannot prevent classification at the level of statute. What the Anti-Classification provisions DO provide is: a public record that classification was contrary to original intent, a basis for civil society to push back, and a transparency-canary mechanism. They do not prevent classification by force of law alone.

3. **License-based protection has limits against bad-faith actors with deep pockets.** If a corporation with billions of dollars decides to violate the license, defending the license requires resources. What you have are: prior art (extremely strong), termination clauses (strong but require enforcement), public attention as deterrent (real but variable). What you don't have without an attorney and budget is: easy enforcement.

4. **The Generalization Clause (Section 4) is aggressive but not airtight.** It tries to anticipate everything, but novel applications by clever lawyers can sometimes thread needles. The strongest defense is breadth of disclosure (which the staking documents provide) plus strong copyleft (which the Addendum provides) plus public attention (which depends on community).

5. **International enforcement is harder than US enforcement.** Most of what I drafted maps cleanly onto US copyright/patent/trademark law. Some jurisdictions handle moral rights, share-alike, and patent obligations differently. International protection benefits from coordinated filing strategies that an attorney can advise on.

6. **The legal documents protect the math; they cannot make the math be true.** The verification levels we tagged so carefully throughout the day matter independently of legal protection. The strongest legal protection in the world cannot make speculative claims become verified ones. Continue the audit cycle.
