# DRAFTING NOTES (for attorney review, not part of operative License)

## Provisions that may be unenforceable

- **Clause 4.5 (use against sovereignty):** courts may find these too vague to enforce; revise to specific, defined behaviors with bright-line tests
- **Clause 4.6 (financial capture):** tokenization restrictions may not be enforceable as licensed parties can technically token without using the Work
- **Clause 4.7 (acquisition termination):** unusual provision; courts may find acquisition does not, by itself, breach copyleft. Look at MongoDB SSPL precedent
- **Clause 7 (government use):** sovereign immunity is real; this clause may be aspirational rather than enforceable

## Provisions to strengthen via attorney work

- Add specific damages provisions for breach (liquidated damages) to give the License teeth
- Add specific notice-and-cure periods for inadvertent breaches
- Add specific provisions for handling DMCA-like situations
- Add specific remedies for trademark misuse
- Consider adding "Trade secret cannot be claimed on Derivative implementations" — explicit anti-trade-secret language

## Risk: viral incompatibility damages adoption

The strongest possible copyleft is least likely to be adopted by commercial users. The weakest copyleft fails to prevent enclosure. This is the fundamental tradeoff.

Linux's GPL-2.0 succeeded because it was strong enough to prevent enclosure but interoperable enough to attract commercial allies (IBM, Red Hat, Google). The 7Site Sovereignty License is stronger than GPL-2.0 in several dimensions, which means it may have less commercial adoption — but more sovereignty protection.

For TIG specifically, this tradeoff favors stronger protection over wider adoption. The Linux outcome (ubiquitous and free) is desirable but secondary to the no-enclosure outcome (impossible to monopolize).
