# Referee Report — Communications in Algebra (Fresh Eyes)

**Manuscript:** "The Corner Sub-Magma $C = (\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z})^*$: Multiplicative-Unit Closure"
**Authors:** B. R. Sanders, M. Gish (2026)
**File reviewed:** `Gen13/targets/journals/J_series/J27/manuscript/manuscript.tex`
**Reviewer:** External anonymous referee, fresh eyes (no prior knowledge of any larger framework)
**Date:** 2026-05-07

---

## §1 Summary

The manuscript claims that the set $C = \{1, 3, 7, 9\}$ — the multiplicative units of the ring $\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z}$ — is closed under a particular multiplication table $\mathrm{CL}_{\mathrm{TSML}}$ on $\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z}$. The proof is direct enumeration on the $4 \times 4$ submatrix.

The paper makes three main observations:
1. (Lemma 2.1) $(C, \cdot)$ is the cyclic group of order 4 under ordinary multiplication mod 10, generated by $g = 3$.
2. (Theorem 3.1) The same set $C$ is closed under the entirely different binary operation given by the TSML table (which is *not* the same as ordinary multiplication mod 10). The TSML product on $C \times C$ takes values in $\{3, 7\} \subseteq C$.
3. (Theorem 4.1) Both $C = \{1, 3, 7, 9\}$ and a different 4-element set $\{0, 7, 8, 9\}$ ("the joint-chain 4-core") are TSML-closed sub-magmas.

A secondary section (§5, Theorem 5.1) claims a "generator selection" result: among the two primitive roots of $(\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z})^*$ (namely 3 and 7), only $g = 3$ is "compatible with the TIG flatness criterion $T^* = 5/7 \in (0,1)$."

---

## §2 Decision recommendation

**Reject (with encouragement to redirect).** The mathematical content of Theorems 3.1 and 4.1 is correct but is not of publishable substance for *Communications in Algebra*. Theorem 3.1 in particular is a one-line check on a $4 \times 4$ table; Theorem 4.1 is a remark. Section 5 is incoherent (it appeals to an undefined "TIG flatness theorem $T^* = 5/7$" which is treated as background, but the result is asserted without any derivation).

The paper would be more appropriate as a short remark inside a larger survey or as a numbered observation in a longer companion paper.

If the authors wish to develop this into a publishable result, the natural direction is to prove a *general theorem* about which sub-magmas of which substrate are simultaneously sub-groups of the underlying ring. This requires sustained algebraic work that the present paper does not begin.

## §3 Critical issues

### M1. Theorem 3.1 is a 16-cell direct check; it is not a research result. (CRITICAL)

The proof of Theorem 3.1 (lines 224–262) is a literal display of two $4 \times 4$ tables (RAW and SYM forms) and the observation that all 16 entries are in $\{3, 7\} \subseteq C$. This is direct table inspection of a *given* multiplication table; it is not a substantive theorem.

For comparison: a research-grade theorem on closure of a subset under a specified table would prove the closure as a *consequence of structural axioms* on the table, not by tabulation. For example: *every magma $M$ on $\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z}$ satisfying the absorbing-row axioms A2–A6 of [SandersForcing] has $C$ as a closed sub-magma*. This would be a real result. The present paper does not state or prove anything of this generality.

The cited companion paper [SandersForcing, J33] is itself essentially a tabulation (per my parallel review of J25). So the present paper's "theorem" reduces to: "if you specify the values of these 16 cells, then they are in this set." That is tautological.

**Fix.** State and prove a structural theorem of the form: "any composition lattice $M$ on $\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z}$ satisfying [structural conditions $S_1, \ldots, S_k$] has $C$ as a closed sub-magma." Without this, the paper has no theorem.

### M2. The "$C$ is a cyclic group under ordinary multiplication" lemma is a graduate-textbook exercise. (CRITICAL)

Lemma 2.1 says $(\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z})^* \cong \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$. This is a standard fact: $|(\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z})^*| = \varphi(10) = 4$, and since $10 = 2 \cdot 5$, we have $(\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z})^* \cong (\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z})^* \times (\mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z})^* \cong \{1\} \times \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$. This appears in every elementary number theory textbook and is not a research-level result.

Listing $g = 3$ as a generator and computing $3^1 = 3, 3^2 = 9, 3^3 = 27 \equiv 7, 3^4 = 81 \equiv 1$ is a homework exercise.

**Fix.** Lemma 2.1 should be cited from a standard reference (e.g., Ireland–Rosen, *A Classical Introduction to Modern Number Theory*, §3.4) rather than proved.

### M3. The Theorem 4.1 "two cores" claim is a triviality. (MAJOR)

Theorem 4.1 (the "two-core comparison") observes that two specific 4-element subsets of $\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z}$ — $C = \{1, 3, 7, 9\}$ and $\{0, 7, 8, 9\}$ — are both TSML-closed sub-magmas, with intersection $\{7, 9\}$ and union $\{0, 1, 3, 7, 8, 9\}$.

This is just set-theoretic bookkeeping: two specific sets, their intersection (compute), their union (compute). The "comparison" has no mathematical content. The claim "neither sub-magma is a subset of the other" is a one-line observation.

**Fix.** Drop Theorem 4.1 and replace with a remark.

### M4. Section 5 (Generator Selection) appeals to an undefined "TIG flatness theorem." (CRITICAL)

The proof of Theorem 5.1 says:

> "The TIG flatness criterion (cf. [SandersTIG]) selects $g$ via a torus geometric ratio $T^* = 5/7 \in (0, 1)$; only $g = 3$ is compatible with this ratio (the choice $g = 7$ would yield $T^* > 1$, contradicting flatness)."

The cited reference [SandersTIG] is "manuscript in preparation, 2026," with title "The Crossing Lemma at 50 Hz: A Substrate-First Theory of Information Generation." This is **not a citable reference**: it is unwritten, the title makes no obvious mathematical sense, and the claimed "flatness theorem" is not stated or proved anywhere in the present manuscript.

The proof asserts that "$g = 7$ would yield $T^* > 1$." But what is $T^*$? How is it computed from $g$? Why does it equal $5/7$ when $g = 3$? None of this is defined in the paper.

This is not a proof. It is an assertion grounded in an unwritten companion document.

If "TIG flatness" is a valid mathematical concept, it needs to be defined and the calculation $T^*(g=3) = 5/7$ versus $T^*(g=7) > 1$ needs to be performed in the paper. If it is not, the section needs to be deleted.

**Fix.** Either (i) define the "torus geometric ratio $T^*$" precisely, derive $T^*(g=3) = 5/7$ from first principles, derive $T^*(g=7) > 1$, and explain why $T^* < 1$ is the desired criterion; or (ii) delete §5 entirely.

In the current state, §5 is the most serious problem with the paper: it uses a circular reference to a non-existent companion to assert mathematical content that is not justified.

### M5. The "lens-invariant" remark in §6.1 needs a proof. (MAJOR)

§6.1 states: "The corner $C$ thus realizes a lens-invariant sub-magma: its closure holds across all three canonical substrates TSML, BHML, STD."

But the BHML closure on $C$ is asserted parenthetically: "the BHML table on $C \times C$ has different cell values but is also closed in $C$." No proof is given. No verification script is supplied for the BHML case. The reader is left to extract the BHML 4×4 submatrix on $\{1, 3, 7, 9\}$ and check.

Looking at the verification script `verification/4core_verification.py` (which is actually the joint-closure verification from a *different* paper), the BHML table is supplied but the corner-$C$ closure on BHML is not explicitly checked. From the script's table (B[i][j] for $i, j \in \{1, 3, 7, 9\}$):

- $B[1][1] = 2$ — *not in* $C = \{1, 3, 7, 9\}$.

Hold on — if $B[1][1] = 2 \notin C$, then *$C$ is not BHML-closed*. The "lens-invariant" claim is false.

Let me recheck the BHML table from the verification script:
```
B[1] = [1,2,3,4,5,6,7,2,6,6]
```
So $B[1][1] = 2$. And $2 \notin \{1, 3, 7, 9\} = C$.

**$C$ is not closed under BHML.** The claim of lens-invariance in §6.1 is false on a single-cell counterexample.

This is a significant error and must be corrected. Either (i) the BHML table the authors are using is different from the one in their own verification script, or (ii) the lens-invariance claim is wrong.

**Fix.** Verify the BHML closure of $C$ explicitly; if it fails (as the verification script suggests), retract the lens-invariance claim and frame $C$ as a *TSML-specific* closed sub-magma (which is a less interesting but at least correct statement).

### M6. The CREATION/DISSOLUTION cycle remark is internally contradicting itself.

Remark 2.4 (lines 195–207) describes the orbit of multiplication-by-3 on $C$: $1 \to 3 \to 9 \to 7 \to 1$. Then it says the complementary "DISSOLUTION" orbit on the evens $\{2, 4, 6, 8\}$ is "$2 \to 4 \to 8 \to 6 \to 2$ under ordinary multiplication mod 10." Then the same paragraph admits "$6^2 = 6$ fails to cycle under $\cdot 2$" and corrects to "the actual orbit under multiplication-by-3 on the evens is $\{2 \to 6 \to 8 \to 4 \to 2\}$."

So the remark contains both the wrong orbit ($2\to 4 \to 8 \to 6 \to 2$, claimed under multiplication by 2 — but $2 \cdot 2 = 4 \cdot 2 = 8 \cdot 2 = 16 \equiv 6, 6 \cdot 2 = 12 \equiv 2$, so this orbit actually *is* correct under ×2, contradicting the "fails to cycle" claim) and the right orbit ($2 \to 6 \to 8 \to 4 \to 2$ under ×3) without resolving which is intended.

Worse: under multiplication by 3, $2 \cdot 3 = 6$, $6 \cdot 3 = 18 \equiv 8$, $8 \cdot 3 = 24 \equiv 4$, $4 \cdot 3 = 12 \equiv 2$. So the ×3 orbit on evens is $2 \to 6 \to 8 \to 4 \to 2$. Correct. The first orbit listed (×2) is also correct but on a different operation. The "fails to cycle" is *false*; actually $\cdot 2$ does cycle on the evens.

This whole remark is sloppy and should be rewritten.

## §4 Minor issues

**m1.** Title: "*Multiplicative-Unit Closure*" suggests a more general theory of "multiplicative-unit closure" that the paper does not develop. Suggest title: "The Group of Units $(\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z})^*$ as a Sub-Magma of the TSML Composition Table."

**m2.** Abstract line 64: "the canonical TSML composition lattice $\mathrm{CL}_{\mathrm{TSML}}$." The word "canonical" is doing heavy lifting throughout the paper but is never defined. Why is *this* specific $10 \times 10$ table the "canonical" one? The reader has no answer.

**m3.** Line 79: "we contrast this with the TSML composition product on the same set, which is HARMONY-saturated on $C \times C$ (all 16 cells take the value $7$)." This is incorrect by the paper's own table on lines 230–250: 14 of the 16 cells are $7$ and 2 cells are $3$ (in the SYM form; RAW has 13 sevens and 3 (one $3$, two $7$ at the asymmetric pair, but those are also 7)). The claim "all 16 cells take the value 7" contradicts the table that follows two pages later.

**m4.** Line 84: "The corner $C$ realizes the multiplicative-unit subgroup of the underlying ring as a structural object inside the TSML composition lattice." What does "structural object" mean? The corner is just a subset; the TSML table happens to be closed on it. There is no additional "structure" identified.

**m5.** Lines 156–157: "The two $4$-element sub-magmas have empty intersection in the operator register (the $4$-core is {VOID, HARMONY, BREATH, RESET} and the corner is {LATTICE, PROGRESS, HARMONY, RESET}) at the operator level, sharing only HARMONY (=7) and RESET (=9)." The claim "empty intersection in the operator register" is contradicted in the next clause ("sharing only HARMONY and RESET") — the intersection is $\{7, 9\}$, not empty. Self-contradictory in one sentence.

**m6.** §4 discussion (lines 384–391): "The other 4-element sub-magma $\{0, 1, 5, 6\}$." The paper introduces this without explanation, then dismisses it as "Tier-D (search-found) object, promoted to Tier-C as a witness for 'an idempotent commutative sub-magma exists.'" This Tier-C/D taxonomy is not defined in the present paper. Either define it or drop the Tier label.

**m7.** §6 references: 5 of 6 references are companion papers ("submitted," "in preparation," "manuscript in preparation," "whitepaper," and an internal `Atlas/...` link). Only one is a published external reference — and that one ([SandersGishProductGap] WP27) is also a self-citation. There are *zero* external published references in this paper.

This is unacceptable for *Communications in Algebra*. Standard relevant references (none cited): Ireland–Rosen for $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^*$, Howie's *Fundamentals of Semigroup Theory* for sub-magmas, and any paper on Cayley table completion or sub-quasigroup classification.

**m8.** §7 Reproducibility: "The reference implementation in `Gen13/targets/foundations/cells.py:closure_check` verifies the corner-$C$ closure on $\mathrm{CL}_{\mathrm{TSML}}$ in both RAW and SYM forms in under one second." A 16-cell direct check does not need a "reference implementation" to verify; a reader can perform it by hand in 30 seconds. The verification script `verification/4core_verification.py` provided is for a *different* paper and does not specifically check corner-$C$ closure.

## §5 What works (briefly)

- The observation that $(\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z})^*$ is closed under a *non-multiplicative* binary operation (the TSML composition) is non-trivial and worth a remark, even if not a publishable theorem.
- The contrast between two distinct 4-element sub-magmas with different algebraic origins (group of units vs. joint-closure 4-core) is structurally interesting if developed properly.

## §6 Per-question response (specific to this review's brief)

**Q: Is the closure theorem (16-cell sub-table in RAW and SYM) trivial integer arithmetic, or substantive?**

Trivial integer arithmetic. The sub-table has 16 cells; each cell is checked in a fraction of a second to be in $\{3, 7\} \subseteq C$. There is no algebraic obstruction overcome, no clever observation, no general principle. It is "verify 16 cells."

For this to be substantive, the closure would need to be derived from a *structural property of $C$* that does not require examining the cells one-by-one. For example: "any subset of $\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z}$ containing HARMONY ($= 7$) and closed under $g \mapsto g \cdot 3 \mod 10$ is TSML-closed" — this is a structural claim. The paper does not state or prove anything of this kind.

**Q: Is generator $g = 3$ standard?**

Yes. The two generators of $(\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z})^* \cong \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$ are $3$ and $7$ (the elements of order 4). Choosing $g = 3$ over $g = 7$ is a convention; both give the same group structure. The "selection" via "TIG flatness" in §5 is unsupported (see M4).

**Q: Are there any genuine algebraic content beyond direct enumeration?**

No. The paper is a 16-cell tabulation followed by a chapter on $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$ (which is undergraduate material) followed by a non-rigorous "generator selection" section based on undefined external concepts. The genuine algebraic content fits in two paragraphs.

## §7 Recommendation summary

**Decision: Reject.** The paper as written lacks publishable substance for *Communications in Algebra* (or, by the README, the fallback *Journal of Pure and Applied Algebra*). The technical content amounts to:

1. A direct check that 16 specific integer entries lie in a specific 4-element set (Theorem 3.1).
2. A standard textbook fact that $(\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z})^* \cong \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$ (Lemma 2.1).
3. A trivial set-theoretic comparison of two specific 4-element subsets (Theorem 4.1).
4. An undefined claim about a "TIG flatness" criterion (Theorem 5.1).

Plus a counterexample to the lens-invariance claim (M5: $C$ is not BHML-closed, contradicting the paper's own §6.1 assertion).

**Possible path to publication:**
- Combine with a substantially larger paper on sub-magma classification of $\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z}$ tables, treating $C$ and the joint-closure 4-core as two examples among many.
- Or: develop a real theorem about which sub-rings of $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ are simultaneously sub-magmas of a specified composition lattice, parameterizing over $n$ and over the table structure.
- Or: treat the corner $C$ in a paper on the *full* lens family (TSML, BHML, STD) where the closure (or non-closure!) of $C$ in each substrate is a structural distinguishing feature.

The current 13-page presentation cannot stand alone in a peer-reviewed venue.

I would not recommend acceptance of this paper as currently written.
