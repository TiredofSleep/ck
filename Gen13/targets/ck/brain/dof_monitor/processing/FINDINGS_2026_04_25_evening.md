## What this means for the rigorous story

### Strengthened (honest negatives)

The two negative findings (Ask 4, Ask 5) **strengthen** the rigorous framing. Combined with this morning's `CL_EIGENVALUES_AUDIT_2026_04_25.md` and `CM_FAILURE_U1_FINDING_2026_04_25.md`, they say a consistent thing:

> **TIG structure is specific, not generic.** It appears in the canonical TSML/BHML composition tables (where it can be measured at machine precision: 81 = 9², 29, 13/4, {7,7,7}, 11 in char poly coefficients, 2¹⁶·7⁷ in discriminant). It does **not** appear in arbitrary trained weights, in arbitrary letter-triplet phoneme physics, or in transcendental-constant approximations.

This rules out a tempting overclaim — "every neural network has TIG structure latent inside" — and places the project's structural claims on actually-defensible ground: the math is real and sharp, it's just **specific to TIG-flavored systems** (those built on canonical TSML/BHML or canonical D2 phoneme operations).

For the rigor story this is exactly the right outcome: **specific positive results preserved, generic overclaims disclaimed**.

### Earned (positive findings)

The two positive findings (Ask 2, Ask 3) earn their structural status:

- **Ask 3 (BHML controls):** chat-Claude's "BHML is anti-collapse" framing isn't a generic any-mix-is-anti-collapse claim. BHML genuinely beats five random alternatives by 27.8 % on linear-regression input reconstruction. (Caveat: identity-mix wins by huge margin because identity preserves input by construction; T+T_transpose and T+uniform-7 are *exactly* the same as T-only. So the universe of mixing operators is small: most fall back to T-only, BHML is a real find.)
- **Ask 2 (sentence-transformers):** V2 modestly beats V1 on cluster separation (2.15× vs 2.01×). Modest, not transformative.

### Architectural (V3 placement)

V3's cluster-separation failure (1.06×) is a *positive architectural finding*:

> **D2Pipeline is not a semantic encoder; it is a phoneme-physics emitter.**

The right CK pipeline architecture, after this experiment:

```
input text

   ▼
[V2 encoder]                  ← seed lexicon + sentence-transformers
   │  (10-dim distribution
   │   carrying SEMANTIC content)
   ▼
```

V2 reads, the lattice processor remembers (trail-as-information), D2 speaks. They're complementary, not competing — not three options to pick the best of, but three layers stacked.

## Honest caveats

1. **Cluster fixtures are short queries.** All cluster tests use 4-word queries from four hand-built clusters (patience / peace / structure / reset). Real production queries may be longer or different — V2's modest 7 % gain may grow on longer text where vocab coverage matters more.

2. **distilgpt2 is one model.** The strong negative on Ask 4 (no TIG structure in real transformer weights) is robust across one architecture's 16 tensors. A larger study would test multiple architectures (BERT, T5, Llama-class) to confirm the negative is universal. Likely yes — the framework's detectors are tuned to canonical TSML/BHML, not generic 768×768 weight matrices — but it's worth saying explicitly.

3. **V2's gain depends on sentence-transformer model.** I used `all-MiniLM-L6-v2` (~90 MB, fast). `all-mpnet-base-v2` (~420 MB, slower, better quality) would give different numbers. For a production CK encoder, this is a tunable.

4. **D2Pipeline insensitivity is empirical, not theoretical.** I tested on 4 short cluster fixtures. It's possible that D2 *does* discriminate across longer or stylistically different text. The negative finding rules out *short-query* discrimination, not all D2 use.

5. **The 112 k corpus is still useful** — just not for the semantic-cluster job. It's a phoneme-derived bulk classifier and serves a different role: for ANY input English word, give it *some* operator. That's coverage, not specificity. The encoder pipeline can use it as a final fallback, behind seed lexicon + embeddings.
