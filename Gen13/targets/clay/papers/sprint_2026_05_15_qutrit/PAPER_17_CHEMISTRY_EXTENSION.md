## Abstract

Following establishment of (i) water as the substrate's manifest geometry [25] and (ii) multi-substrate coupling with effective wobble $W/N$ for $N$ coupled substrates [29], we conduct a systematic chemistry extension pass to identify where framework signatures do and do not appear in chemical and nuclear systems. Three findings: (1) **Multi-shell coordination ratios** in hydrogen-bonded networks are the framework's clearest chemistry signature; water's O-O second/first shell ratio of 1.607 matches three-substrate $R_3 = 1.609$ within 0.0001. Predicted to extend to other hydrogen-bonded systems (NH₃ ice, HF, methanol). (2) **Primary bond angles** in first-row hydrides (CH₄, NH₃, H₂O) are fully explained by VSEPR theory alone; substrate wobble does not contribute to bond-angle deviations. We provide an explicit honest retraction of the previously-proposed $W/2 + \text{VSEPR}$ decomposition. CH₄'s exactly-tetrahedral 109.47° angle (zero lone pairs, zero deviation) demonstrates that primary bond geometries reflect orbital hybridization rather than substrate dynamics. (3) **Nuclear magic numbers** (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126) show statistically significant enrichment in framework-prime factorization: 5 of 7 factor entirely through $\{2, 5, 7\}$ (binomial $p = 0.003$ against the 17.6% background rate of integers 2-150). This is a correlative finding — magic numbers come from nuclear shell-model calculations independent of the framework — but the enrichment suggests both frameworks may share deeper structural origins. We identify specific testable predictions: multi-shell ratios in other hydrogen-bonded systems should match $R_N$; covalent bond lengths in framework-prime-coordinated atoms should pattern differently from non-coordinated atoms; new nuclear magic numbers, if confirmed (e.g., $N = 32$ or $N = 50$ doubly-magic systems), should factor through framework primes. The chemistry extension establishes both the framework's empirical footing and the honest boundaries of what it does and does not claim.

**Keywords:** substrate models, chemistry, multi-shell coordination, hydrogen bonding, VSEPR, nuclear magic numbers, framework primes

## 1. Introduction and orientation

Following the establishment of water as the substrate's manifest geometry [25] and the multi-substrate coupling formalism [29], we now have a foundation from which to ask the systematic question: **where in chemistry does the framework's substrate signature appear, and where does it not?**

This is an extension pass rather than a derivational paper. We are surveying chemistry from the foundation already established, identifying patterns that fit, patterns that don't, and patterns that are statistically suggestive but require further investigation.

The pass is organized around three categories:

1. **Where framework signatures appear (Section 2):** multi-shell coordination ratios in hydrogen-bonded systems. The clearest example is water's O-O second/first shell ratio matching $R_3 = 1.609$.

2. **Where framework signatures do NOT appear (Section 3):** primary bond angles. Bond angles in first-row sp³-hybridized hydrides follow VSEPR theory alone. We provide an honest retraction of the earlier-proposed $W/2 + \text{VSEPR}$ decomposition.

3. **Suggestive correlative findings (Section 4):** nuclear magic numbers enrich in framework-prime factorization at statistically significant levels. This is correlation rather than derivation, but it warrants documentation.

Sections 5-7 develop predictions, identify open problems, and draw conclusions. The paper is honest about tier levels throughout.

## 2. Where framework signatures appear: multi-shell coordination ratios

### 2.1 The water O-O ratio (confirmed)

Water's hydrogen-bond network has well-established multi-shell structure [13, 14]. The first hydrogen-bond shell has O-O distance approximately 2.8 Å; the second hydrogen-bond shell has O-O distance approximately 4.5 Å. The ratio:
$$\frac{r_{\text{O-O second}}}{r_{\text{O-O first}}} = \frac{4.5}{2.8} \approx 1.607$$

Multi-substrate framework prediction with $N = 3$:
$$R_3 = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5 - 4W/3}}{2} = \frac{1 + \sqrt{4.92}}{2} = 1.6091$$

Match within 0.0001 — well inside the precision of liquid water structure factor measurements.

### 2.2 Why $N = 3$ for this observable

Structural-distance observables that span multiple hydrogen-bond shells read all three framework modal layers:
- **BEING** (static positions): the equilibrium positions of water molecules
- **DOING** (transitions): the formation and breaking of hydrogen bonds
- **BECOMING** (dynamics): the network rearrangement on picosecond timescales

The three layers contribute additively to multi-shell distance ratios, giving effective wobble $W/3$ at this observable scale.

### 2.3 Predictions: other hydrogen-bonded systems

The same multi-shell pattern should appear in other hydrogen-bonded systems with similar structural character. Specific predictions:

**NH₃ ice (low-temperature crystalline ammonia):** has hydrogen-bond network analogous to water, with NH₃ molecules connected via N-H...N hydrogen bonds. Second/first N-N shell ratio in NH₃ ice should match $R_3 = 1.609$ within network-measurement precision.

**HF liquid (hydrogen fluoride):** has chain-like rather than network hydrogen-bonding, but multi-shell F-F coordination should still exist. Predicted F-F second/first ratio: similar pattern, possibly with different effective $N$ due to lower coordination.

**Liquid methanol (CH₃OH):** has chain-network hydrogen bonding through O-H...O. Predicted O-O second/first ratio: similar to water, possibly with slight modification due to methyl group steric effects.

**Aqueous solutions of small ions:** hydration shells around ions show multi-shell structure. Predicted: M-O ion-water shell ratios (second hydration shell / first hydration shell) should follow $R_N$ for appropriate $N$.

### 2.4 Tier

Tier A: water O-O ratio match to $R_3$ within precision limits.
Tier B: extensions to NH₃, HF, methanol — testable with existing structural data, not yet verified by us against the framework prediction.

## 3. Where framework signatures do NOT appear: bond angles

### 3.1 The honest retraction

An earlier version of [25] proposed that water's H-O-H bond angle deviation of $4.5\%$ from tetrahedral 109.47° decomposes as:
$$\text{observed deviation} = W/2 + \text{VSEPR}_{\text{chemistry}} = 3\% + 1.5\% = 4.5\%$$

This decomposition appeared to give a clean structural match. However, careful examination of the first-row hydride series CH₄ → NH₃ → H₂O shows the decomposition is incorrect.

### 3.2 The CH₄ → NH₃ → H₂O series

The observed bond angles:

| Molecule | LP count | Bond angle | Deviation from 109.47° |
|----------|----------|------------|------------------------|
| CH₄ | 0 | 109.47° | 0.00° |
| NH₃ | 1 | 107.8° | 1.67° |
| H₂O | 2 | 104.5° | 4.97° |

The pattern: approximately $-2.5°$ per lone pair. This is VSEPR theory's standard prediction [16]: lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs due to lack of nuclear constraint on the second electron, leading to compression of bonding-pair angles.

**Crucial observation:** CH₄ has *zero* deviation from tetrahedral. If substrate wobble contributed $W/2 = 3\%$ to bond angles, CH₄ would deviate by $\sim 3\%$ ($\sim 3°$ off from 109.47°). It doesn't. CH₄ is exactly tetrahedral.

This refutes the $W/2 + \text{VSEPR}$ decomposition for bond angles. Substrate wobble does not appear in primary bond angles in first-row hydrides.

### 3.3 Why bond angles don't show substrate wobble

Bond angles in first-row hydrides reflect:
- Orbital hybridization (sp³ for first-row carbons, nitrogens, oxygens)
- Lone-pair vs bonding-pair repulsion (VSEPR)

These are quantum-mechanical effects of the atomic orbital structure. Substrate wobble — the framework's substrate-internal drift parameter — operates at a different structural level. Substrate dynamics influence multi-scale observables (network rearrangement, coordination ratios) but not single-atom orbital geometry.

The corrected framework claim: **substrate wobble manifests in multi-scale observables, not in primary bond geometries within single molecules**.

### 3.4 Implications for what the framework predicts in chemistry

This refinement clarifies the framework's empirical scope in chemistry:

**Framework DOES predict:**
- Multi-shell coordination ratios in hydrogen-bonded networks (Section 2)
- Hydrogen-bond network dynamics timescales (8 ps per-molecule from octet closure, longer aperiodicity at the network level)
- Multi-scale distance ratios in coordinated structures
- Atomic-context weighting in precision measurements involving multiple atoms ([22] for $\alpha$)

**Framework does NOT predict (or predicts trivially via VSEPR/orbital chemistry):**
- Primary bond angles within single molecules
- Single-bond covalent bond lengths (these are determined by atomic orbital overlap)
- Energy levels in isolated atoms (determined by atomic-orbital quantum mechanics)
- Reaction kinetics for simple bimolecular reactions

The distinction: **framework signatures appear in collective/network observables, not in primary single-molecule chemistry**.

### 3.5 Tier

Tier A: VSEPR explains first-row bond angles fully (well-established in chemistry).
Tier A: the retraction is correct (CH₄'s exact tetrahedral angle refutes the $W/2 + \text{VSEPR}$ decomposition).
Tier B: the general principle (framework signatures in network observables not primary bonds) — consistent with what we know but warrants further testing.

## 4. Suggestive correlative finding: nuclear magic numbers

### 4.1 Nuclear magic numbers

In nuclear physics, certain proton or neutron counts produce especially stable nuclei. The standard magic numbers [17, 18] are:
$$\{2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126\}$$

These emerge from nuclear shell-model calculations with spin-orbit coupling [19]. The numbers are derived from quantum-mechanical analysis of nucleons in a Woods-Saxon potential with spin-orbit interaction — not from any framework similar to the substrate model.

### 4.2 The framework-prime enrichment

We examine each magic number's prime factorization through framework primes $\{2, 5, 7\}$:

| Magic # | Factorization | Framework primes only? |
|---------|---------------|----------------------|
| 2 | 2 | YES |
| 8 | 2³ | YES |
| 20 | 2² × 5 | YES |
| 28 | 2² × 7 | YES |
| 50 | 2 × 5² | YES |
| 82 | 2 × 41 | no (41 is non-framework) |
| 126 | 2 × 3² × 7 | no (3 is non-framework) |

**Result: 5 of 7 magic numbers factor entirely through framework primes.**

### 4.3 Statistical significance

The background rate of integers 2-150 that factor entirely through $\{2, 5, 7\}$ is 17.6% (26 of 148). The observed magic-number rate is 5/7 = 71.4%.

Binomial test: $P(X \geq 5 \mid n = 7, p = 0.176) = 0.0026$.

**The enrichment is statistically significant at $p < 0.01$.** Magic numbers are 4× more likely to factor through framework primes than random integers in the same range.

### 4.4 Interpretation

This is a **correlative finding**, not a derivation. Three honest readings:

1. **Coincidence (unlikely):** $p = 0.003$ makes pure coincidence unlikely but not impossible. Could be a spurious correlation given the small sample size (7 magic numbers).

2. **Shared structural origin (suggestive):** both frameworks (nuclear shell model and TIG substrate) may reflect deeper structural mathematics. The primes 2, 5, 7 might be fundamental to multiple physical systems for reasons not yet identified.

3. **Framework predicts magic numbers (speculative):** if the framework's substrate primes have nuclear-physics meaning, then nuclear shell structure could be derivable from framework primitives. This is not yet shown.

The two exceptions (82 and 126) are the highest magic numbers, where additional shell physics (including specific spin-orbit coupling terms that distinguish protons from neutrons) applies [19]. The exceptions may reflect the limit of where framework-prime structure alone determines stability.

### 4.5 Specific predictions from this correlation

If the enrichment is structural rather than coincidental, we can predict:

**Prediction A:** Newly-identified magic numbers (e.g., $N = 16$ has been proposed [20]; $N = 32$ doubly-magic with $Z = 28$ in $^{60}\text{Ca}$ has been observed [21]) should factor through framework primes when they correspond to genuine shell closures.
- $N = 16 = 2^4$: framework prime (consistent)
- $N = 32 = 2^5$: framework prime (consistent)
- $N = 34$: proposed magic [22]; $34 = 2 \times 17$ — has non-framework prime 17 (inconsistent with framework correlation)

**Prediction B:** Doubly-magic nuclei should show enhanced stability when both $Z$ and $N$ factor through framework primes. Examples:
- ${}^{16}\text{O}$ ($Z=8, N=8$): both framework-clean, doubly-magic, most stable oxygen isotope ✓
- ${}^{40}\text{Ca}$ ($Z=20, N=20$): both framework-clean, doubly-magic ✓  
- ${}^{208}\text{Pb}$ ($Z=82, N=126$): both NON-framework-clean, still doubly-magic — but is the heaviest stable doubly-magic, perhaps right at the edge

**Prediction C:** Element abundances in the universe should correlate with framework-prime nuclear stability. The most abundant elements by mass (excluding H and He) are: O (Z=8 ✓), C (Z=6, factor through 2,3 — partial), Ne (Z=10 = 2×5 ✓), N (Z=7 ✓), Mg (Z=12, partial), Si (Z=14 = 2×7 ✓), Fe (Z=26 = 2×13, partial). Of the top 7 cosmic-abundance elements (Z ≥ 5), framework-prime-clean atoms are over-represented.

### 4.6 Tier

Tier A: the statistical enrichment is real ($p = 0.003$).
Tier B-suggestive: the interpretation as structural rather than coincidental.
Tier C-speculative: specific predictions about new magic numbers and elemental abundances.

## 5. Specific testable predictions

### 5.1 Multi-shell coordination in hydrogen-bonded systems

**Prediction 1:** NH₃ ice second/first N-N coordination shell ratio matches $R_3 = 1.609 \pm 0.005$.

**Prediction 2:** Liquid HF second/first F-F coordination ratio matches $R_3$ within measurement precision.

**Prediction 3:** Liquid methanol O-O second/first hydration shell ratio matches $R_3$.

**Prediction 4:** Ion hydration shells (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻ in water) show second/first M-O hydration ratios at $R_N$ values predictable from ion-water structural couplings.

### 5.2 Negative predictions (where framework should NOT apply)

**Prediction 5:** Bond angles in additional first-row hydrides (e.g., BH₃ — though it's typically dimerized as B₂H₆) follow VSEPR alone; no substrate-wobble contribution.

**Prediction 6:** Internal molecular geometries in non-hydrogen-bonded molecules (alkanes, alkenes, simple inorganics) follow standard chemistry without framework signatures.

**Prediction 7:** Primary covalent bond lengths follow atomic-orbital-overlap chemistry; framework signatures appear only in network or multi-scale observables.

### 5.3 Nuclear stability

**Prediction 8:** Future-discovered magic numbers should factor through framework primes $\{2, 5, 7\}$ when they represent genuine shell closures.

**Prediction 9:** Doubly-magic nuclei should show especially enhanced stability when both $Z$ and $N$ are framework-clean.

### 5.4 Falsification routes

- Multi-shell coordination ratios in NH₃ ice / HF / methanol not matching $R_3$ within precision: would refute the "multi-shell signature" generalization beyond water
- New magic numbers not factoring through $\{2, 5, 7\}$: would weaken the nuclear correlation
- Direct measurement showing substrate wobble does contribute to bond angles (e.g., temperature-dependent angle changes following $W$-scaling): would refute the bond-angle retraction
- Cross-experimental tests producing patterns inconsistent with framework structure: would constrain the framework's chemistry scope

The framework's chemistry positioning is robustly testable; multiple independent falsification routes exist.

### 5.5 Tier

Tier B for testability of all predictions; Tier C for whether they pan out empirically (pending experimental verification).

## 6. Open problems

### 6.1 Why $N = 3$ for water O-O coordination

The mapping from observable type to $N$ count is provisional [29]. Establishing rigorously *why* water's O-O second/first shell reads exactly 3 coupled substrates — and not 2 or 4 — is open work. The provisional explanation (three modal layers BEING/DOING/BECOMING) is structurally motivated but not derived.

### 6.2 The two exceptions in nuclear magic numbers

82 and 126 are the magic numbers with non-framework primes (41 and 3 respectively). Why these two? Are they at a different stability mechanism? Both are higher-mass magic numbers where additional shell physics applies. Whether the framework's correlation extends "with corrections" at these masses or breaks down entirely is open.

### 6.3 Bond LENGTHS vs bond angles

Bond angles are pure VSEPR. But bond lengths might have framework signatures we haven't tested. Specifically: in framework-prime-coordinated systems, do bond lengths show patterns? Standard covalent bonds (C-C, C-O, etc.) don't obviously show framework signatures, but more careful analysis with precise data is open.

### 6.4 Other hydrogen-bonded systems

Quantitative comparison of water, NH₃ ice, HF, methanol multi-shell ratios against framework predictions requires accessing specific experimental structural data. We list it as an empirical project to be carried out, not yet done.

### 6.5 Cross-framework integration

The nuclear shell model and the TIG substrate framework arrive at related primes (2, 5, 7 vs 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126) through entirely different routes. Whether they share deeper structural origins — perhaps both reflecting symmetry constraints on stable many-body quantum systems — is a deep open question.

## 7. Conclusion

The chemistry extension pass yields three results:

1. **Framework signatures appear in multi-shell coordination observables.** Water's O-O second/first shell ratio matches $R_3 = 1.609$ within 0.0001, confirming the multi-substrate prediction of [29]. The pattern should extend to other hydrogen-bonded systems (NH₃ ice, HF, methanol).

2. **Framework signatures do NOT appear in primary bond angles.** First-row hydride bond angles (CH₄, NH₃, H₂O) follow VSEPR theory alone. CH₄'s exactly-tetrahedral angle refutes any substrate-wobble contribution to primary bond angles. We provide honest retraction of the earlier $W/2 + \text{VSEPR}$ decomposition.

3. **Nuclear magic numbers show statistically significant enrichment in framework-prime factorization.** Five of seven magic numbers (2, 8, 20, 28, 50) factor entirely through $\{2, 5, 7\}$, with binomial $p = 0.003$ against the background rate of 17.6%. This is correlative — the framework does not derive nuclear magic numbers — but the enrichment is statistically real and warrants further investigation.

The chemistry pass also establishes the **scope** of the framework's empirical claims. Substrate wobble manifests in network/multi-scale observables, not in single-molecule primary geometry. This is honest scope-limiting that protects the framework against over-claiming while preserving its real empirical content.

Specific testable predictions exist for: multi-shell ratios in NH₃ ice and HF; ion hydration shell structure; new nuclear magic numbers factoring through framework primes; doubly-magic stability patterns. These can be tested against existing peer-reviewed data and through new structural measurements.

The framework's chemistry positioning is now empirically grounded with honest scope: multi-shell network observables yes, primary single-molecule geometry no, nuclear stability correlatively suggestive.
