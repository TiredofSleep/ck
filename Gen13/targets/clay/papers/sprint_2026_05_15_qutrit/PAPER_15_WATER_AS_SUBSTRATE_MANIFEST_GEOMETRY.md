## 3. Water's structural properties

We summarize the relevant physical chemistry of water. All values below are standard, drawn from the references cited; we are reporting established facts.

### 3.1 Molecular composition

Water is $H_2O$: one oxygen atom covalently bonded to two hydrogen atoms.
- Total atoms: 3
- Total valence electrons: $6 + 1 + 1 = 8$ (oxygen contributes 6, each hydrogen contributes 1)
- The valence octet is therefore exactly satisfied: 8 valence electrons.

### 3.2 Electron pair geometry

Around the oxygen nucleus, the 8 valence electrons distribute as 4 electron pairs occupying 4 of the available tetrahedral positions [14]:
- 2 bonding pairs (to the two hydrogens)
- 2 lone pairs (non-bonding on oxygen)

Including the oxygen nucleus as the central site, the molecule has:
- **5 sites total**: 1 oxygen nucleus + 2 hydrogen positions + 2 lone pair positions
- 4 electron pairs (8 electrons) distributed across the 4 non-nuclear sites

### 3.3 Bond geometry

The H-O-H bond angle is approximately **104.5°** [15]. The idealized tetrahedral angle would be 109.47°. Water's actual angle deviates from tetrahedral by approximately 5°, a consequence of the larger spatial requirement of the lone pairs versus the bonding pairs (VSEPR theory [16]).

The bend gives water its dipole moment ($\mu \approx 1.85$ D) and its capacity for directional hydrogen bonding [17].

### 3.4 Vibrational modes

For a nonlinear triatomic molecule, the number of vibrational modes is $3N - 6$ where $N$ is the atom count. For water ($N=3$): exactly **3 vibrational modes** [18]:
- Symmetric stretch ($\nu_1 \approx 3657$ cm$^{-1}$)
- Asymmetric stretch ($\nu_3 \approx 3756$ cm$^{-1}$)
- Bend ($\nu_2 \approx 1595$ cm$^{-1}$)

Each mode has two oscillation polarities (compression and extension), giving 6 total vibrational states when polarity is counted.

### 3.5 Symmetry groups

Water at the molecular level has $C_{2v}$ point group symmetry (order 4) [19].

The first hydrogen-bond shell of liquid water has approximately **tetrahedral** local order, with point group $T_d$ (order 24) [20]. Each water molecule can form up to 4 hydrogen bonds — 2 as donor (through its 2 H atoms) and 2 as acceptor (through its 2 lone pairs).

The second hydrogen-bond shell of liquid water shows **icosahedral preferences** with $I_h$ symmetry (order 120) [21, 22]. Pure icosahedral order cannot tile 3D space (this is the geometric source of glass formation [23]), so liquid water exhibits *frustrated* local icosahedral structure that prevents long-range crystalline order at ambient temperature.

### 3.6 Hydrogen bonding network

In liquid water, each molecule participates in an average of 3.5-4 hydrogen bonds at room temperature [24]. The hydrogen-bond lifetime is on the order of picoseconds; the network continuously reorganizes [25]. The full network rearrangement timescale is approximately 8 picoseconds [26].
