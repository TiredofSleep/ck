# Why This Is the First Real Checkpoint
## Sprint Rationale

---

## What the Foundation Sprint Produced

The four foundation documents landed on a single structural commitment: the primitive for the hold-gap-flow interaction is the pair, not any one aspect. Distinction is the relational object underneath; hold is its persistent-side reading, gap is its excluded-side reading. Flow tests the pair; seam is where the pair becomes visible; TSML/BHML are the first finite grammar in which the pair is operated.

This is a framework, not a finding. It organizes the program's existing pieces under a common ground without asserting any new theorem. Every claim in the foundation note was tagged as suggestive or research-direction, with one exception: the specific already-proven results from Z/10 and the three Path 3 bridge PASSes stayed in the exact tier unchanged.

The framework's first rigorous test is whether it cashes out on data the program already has.

---

## What "Cashes Out" Means Here

A foundation framework that cannot connect to measurable structure in the existing record is worth nothing. The program has resisted frameworks that live purely in interpretive space. The discipline is: every framework must identify at least one place where its structural claims produce a specific, testable prediction against data in hand, with a null model and pre-registered thresholds.

The ADD/MAX subtype mapping question is the first place where this discipline can be applied to the pair primitive. The question has four features that make it a real checkpoint:

1. **It operates on the local theorem chart.** Z/10's 8-cell seam is the program's most rigorously characterized object. The subtype labels (ADD on 2 cells, MAX on 6 cells) are part of the 3-layer tower theorem. If the pair-primitive framework has any purchase at all, it should have it here.
2. **The data already exists.** No new extraction, no new generator, no new pre-registered recovery. The 8 cells are fixed. The ADD/MAX classification is fixed. The three Path 3 bridge findings are on record. The sprint tests a mapping against static data.
3. **There are two concrete candidate mappings, exhaustive under the framework's vocabulary.** Map A: ADD = persistent-side, MAX = excluded-side. Map B: MAX = persistent-side, ADD = excluded-side. No third option exists under the pair-primitive framework because the subtypes are binary and the pair's readings are binary. One mapping fits, or neither fits, or both fit (which would mean the mapping is unforced by the data).
4. **The prediction has specific structural consequences.** Each mapping predicts which subtype should play which role in the three Path 3 PASSes. Identity-element attachment (v1.1), leaf-edge placement (v1.2-adj), and topology-family behavior (P3AP) each bear on the pair's reading in a specific operational lens. A coherent mapping should make all three readings point the same way.

---

## What Makes This Different From Sprint 17's Broader Explorations

Sprint 17 and its companions produced many candidate structural characterizations of Z/10's seam: ring-ranking patterns, MDL considerations, negative results across alternative subtypes, permutation-rep observations. Those explorations are in the working snapshot and are load-bearing for the program's broader shape.

What this checkpoint does that those did not: it frames the question so that the *pair primitive* is the object being tested, not Z/10's seam structure itself. The seam structure is the data; the pair primitive is the hypothesis; the mapping is the operational bridge between them. This is the first sprint where the pair primitive has a well-formed yes/no test.

A failure here does not refute any result in Sprint 17 or the B2 pack. It refutes the pair-primitive framework's claim to organize those results under one ground. A passage here does not upgrade any B2 result to a new status. It confirms that the pair-primitive framework is at least consistent with the first place we could check it.

---

## Why Subtype Mapping Specifically

The foundation note named five candidate central questions:

- Is matter stabilized flow around gaps? (Too physics-forward.)
- Is a prime a hold because it curves absence? (Still in hold-first register, which the foundation sprint explicitly rejected.)
- Is the seam where hold and gap become visible together? (Covered by the foundation note itself.)
- What operation structures admit a pair primitive? (Too abstract; no concrete test.)
- Does the ADD/MAX subtype split correspond to the two readings of the pair primitive? (Narrow, testable, has data in hand.)

Only the last candidate met the program's discipline. It meets it because it is narrow — one ring, one 8-cell seam, one binary question. It is testable — the mapping either fits the existing data or it does not. And it is falsifiable in a structural sense: if neither map A nor map B produces a coherent reading across the four data sources (the 8 cells, v1.1, v1.2-adj, P3AP), the framework cannot claim to have organized the local theorem chart.

---

## Placement in the Program's Register

The B2 closeout pack records 11 sprints in the Z/10 TSML transport program, four substantive PASSes, six informative negatives, one vacuous PASS, one UNCLEAR with documented residual. Every sprint was narrow. Every verdict was pre-registered. Every attribution document explained why the specific metric and null were the right choices for the specific question.

This sprint fits that register exactly:

- It is narrow. One ring, one mapping question.
- Its verdict will be pre-registered. Map A wins / Map B wins / neither wins / both fit.
- Its attribution is this document plus the scope note.

It is not a new research program. It is a single checkpoint. A PASS adds one sentence to the record. A FAIL adds one sentence. An UNCLEAR documents the specific reason the mapping was not decidable from existing data, and the framework proceeds only with explicit acknowledgement of that ambiguity.

---

## Why Now

The foundation sprint's four documents reorganized how the program's pieces relate. That reorganization has structural consequences for how future sprints should be framed. But before any future sprint can be framed under the pair-primitive framework, the framework itself needs its first rigorous test. Otherwise every future sprint would be trying to build on an untested foundation.

The subtype mapping checkpoint is the first moment where:
- The framework makes a specific, binary claim.
- The data to test that claim already exists.
- The test can be done with no new infrastructure.
- The result has a clean interpretation either way.

Running it now — before any other sprint built on the framework — is the conservative move.

---

## What This Checkpoint Does Not Promise

A PASS does not prove the pair primitive is the correct framework. It proves that the framework is consistent with one specific piece of data that it would need to be consistent with. Consistency with one checkpoint is weaker than consistency with many, and much weaker than independent derivation of the framework from first principles.

A FAIL does not prove the pair primitive is wrong. It proves that the framework cannot cleanly organize the local theorem chart's subtype structure. There might be a refinement of the framework — different operational lenses, different pair readings, different mapping schemes — that would pass. But the burden is on any refinement to meet the same discipline the current framework is trying to meet.

An UNCLEAR — where both mappings fit comparably, or neither does, or the data underdetermines the choice — is the most informative of the three outcomes. It would say the pair primitive is neither confirmed nor refuted by the existing seam data; something finer would be needed.

The checkpoint's value is structural regardless of outcome. It gives the pair-primitive framework its first engagement with the program's rigorous register. Whatever it returns, the framework leaves this sprint with either a confirmed point of contact, a specific place to retract, or a specific ambiguity to resolve.

---

## One-Line Justification

This sprint is the first real checkpoint because the pair-primitive framework, until now, has been a coherent reorganization of the program's pieces with no data in hand, and this is the earliest place where the framework can either earn or lose its claim to that organization against a pre-registered test on existing measurements.
