# What A Boundary Checkpoint Would Add Beyond The Seam Subtype Checkpoint
## Short Scope Note

---

## The Distinction

The four PPM sprints (v1.0, v1.1, v2.0, v2.1) tested **one checkpoint** — the subtype-mapping checkpoint — at four positions of a 2×2 design space. The checkpoint itself is a specific question: *does Map B (MAX = persistent, ADD = excluded) fit the seam?*

A V0 boundary checkpoint would be a **different checkpoint** on the same ring. Not the same question at a new scope, but a new question entirely. This is a distinct kind of evidence.

---

## What A Second Independent Checkpoint Would Change

### Before a second checkpoint

The framework has been tested at one structural point: the seam's MAX/ADD partition. A PASS at four positions of this one checkpoint establishes that the framework's vocabulary produces a stable reading of this structural feature. It does not establish that the framework has broader structural content on Z/10 — only that it has consistent content on this one feature.

Under the existing results, plausible readings of the framework include:
- "The framework correctly reads the seam's MAX/ADD partition and may or may not read anything else."
- "The framework is about seam structure specifically, not ring structure broadly."
- "The framework's vocabulary happens to map onto the seam's features but would not map onto other features."

These readings are **all compatible** with what the PPM sprints have earned.

### After a second independent checkpoint

A boundary-checkpoint PASS would rule out the narrow readings. It would establish that the framework's vocabulary makes contact with **at least two structurally distinct features** on Z/10 — seam mapping and boundary behavior. Two independent checkpoints is a different epistemic status than one checkpoint at four positions.

A boundary-checkpoint FAIL would be equally informative in the other direction. It would support the narrow readings: the framework is about seam structure, not ring structure.

Either outcome advances the program's understanding of what the framework is actually doing.

---

## What "Independent" Means Here

Two checkpoints are **independent** if the structural feature one scores is not derivable from the feature the other scores. In this case:

- The seam subtype checkpoint scores MAX/ADD partition of the 8 seam cells.
- The V0 boundary checkpoint would score the 19 V0 cells' zero-absorption pattern with HARMONY exceptions.

These are disjoint regions of the table. The seam is defined on $R^2 \setminus V_0 \setminus \text{shell}$; V0 is the 19 cells where vertex 0 appears. No seam cell is a V0 cell. The checkpoints are operating on structurally disjoint inputs.

Passing both would establish that the framework's vocabulary has coherent content on two disjoint structural features of the same ring. Failing one and passing the other would specify the framework's scope precisely.

---

## Why A Boundary Checkpoint Specifically (vs. Another Seam-Related Checkpoint)

The program could in principle test variant seam checkpoints: different rubric sources, different operationalizations, different cleanness thresholds. But these would all be **elaborations of the same checkpoint**, not independent checkpoints.

A boundary checkpoint is genuinely different because:

1. **Different region of the table.** V0 is disjoint from the seam by construction.
2. **Different operational character.** V0 behaves under additive-identity absorption, a qualitatively distinct regime from the seam's MAX vs ADD choice-making.
3. **Different candidate rubric features.** The boundary features (asymmetry, attractor privilege, exception structure) are not features the PPM rubric tested.
4. **Different vocabulary connection.** The framework's gap/boundary language connects to V0 in a way it does not connect to the seam directly.

Testing the boundary is the narrowest possible way to introduce **structurally independent** evidence without widening to transport, to new operationalizations, or to new generators.

---

## What A Boundary Checkpoint Cannot Do

Even a PASS cannot establish:

- That the framework is ring-structural in general (two points of contact is not a theorem).
- That the framework predicts V0's content rather than accommodating it.
- That the framework extends to any feature not yet tested (BHML, unit structure, higher-order interactions).
- That any prior PPM verdict is strengthened by the V0 result.
- That SAH is supported.

Per the scope discipline inherited from prior PPM sprints:
- Rule 18 (wording clarification): each checkpoint tests a specific operational reading, not the framework in all readings.
- Rule 19 (no composition): v3.0 verdict is its own sentence; no merged claim with v1.0/v1.1/v2.0/v2.1.
- Rule 21 (disclose diagnostic possibilities): honest prediction in the pre-reg.
- Rule 22 (anti-upgrade): inherited context (canonical construction, attractor reconciliation) not re-scored.
- Rule 23 (sub-pattern preservation): FAIL sub-patterns documented.

---

## The Honest Framing Of What V3.0-V0 Adds

> *v3.0-V0 tests whether the pair-primitive framework makes a pre-registered point of contact with V0's boundary behavior on Z/10. A PASS adds a second independent Z/10 checkpoint, ruling out the narrow reading that the framework is seam-specific. A FAIL specifies that the framework's scope on Z/10 is the seam, not the ring broadly. Either outcome is a one-sentence advance over the four-position 2×2 state.*

This is the entirety of what v3.0-V0 adds. Any stronger framing is outside what the sprint can earn.

---

## One-Sentence Statement

A boundary checkpoint would add structurally independent evidence to the program: testing whether the pair-primitive framework has content beyond the seam's MAX/ADD partition, using a disjoint region of Z/10 (V0's 19 cells) with disjoint structural features (zero-absorption with HARMONY exceptions), scored under the same PPM discipline that produced the first four verdicts.
