# What Bridge A Can Show, and What It Cannot Show
## Short Note

---

## What Bridge A Can Show

Bridge A is a pairwise comparison at the graph-topology level between (a) the Path 1 theorem seam on Z/10 and (b) the Path 2 prior-free discovered seams on other carriers. It can answer questions of the form:

- *Do these seams share a topological signature?* Specifically: component count distribution, forest-ness, maximum-degree band, degree-sequence shape.
- *Is that signature distinguishable from random graphs of matching density?* Via an edge-count-preserving null.
- *Which specific features transport, and which do not?* A seam that is tree-shaped on Z/10 and tree-shaped on most Path 2 carriers is evidence of topological family resemblance on that specific feature. A seam that is tree-shaped on Z/10 but cyclic on most Path 2 carriers is evidence against.

Bridge A's strongest positive claim is: **the Path 1 theorem seam and the Path 2 empirical seams share a topological signature that is not reproducible by random graphs of matching density.** That claim is narrow, falsifiable, and supported or refuted by the frozen metrics.

---

## What Bridge A Cannot Show

Bridge A is a structural-overlap test. It does not speak to the content of the seams — only their shape. Several things are explicitly outside its scope:

**Cell identity.** Bridge A does not compare which cells are in each seam. Z/10's seam contains $(2, 4), (4, 2), (4, 8), (8, 4), (2, 9), (9, 2), (1, 2), (2, 1)$ by the theorem. Sprint 21's seam on Z/14 contains some different cells with different numerical labels. Whether those cells "correspond" under any cross-carrier mapping is not a Bridge A question.

**Rule subtypes.** The published Z/10 seam has a declared MAX/ADD partition (six MAX-type cells, two ADD-type cells). The Path 2 seams discovered prior-free do not carry rule labels — they are just cells where the empirical operator deviated from canonical. Bridge A can compare topology but cannot claim the Path 2 seams have the same rule-type composition without a separate rule-classification step. This is why the subtype-mix metric is included only as an optional diagnostic, not as a primary pass criterion.

**Theorem status on non-Z/10 carriers.** A Bridge A pass does not establish that any theorem holds on Path 2 carriers. It establishes a *structural resemblance* between a theorem object on one ring and empirical objects on other rings. Theorem-level claims require theorem-level proofs, which Bridge A does not provide.

**Transport in the stronger sense.** Bridge A does not show that "the theorem's structure transports." It shows that the theorem's seam shares topology features with empirical seams on other carriers. Transport, as a stronger concept, would require demonstrating that specific invariants of the theorem's construction carry over to other carriers' constructions under an explicit mapping — a larger claim than shape resemblance.

**Causation or origin.** If Path 1 and Path 2 seams share topology, Bridge A does not explain why. The resemblance might reflect deep structural truth, or it might reflect that canonical-plus-seam constructions with small dense cores tend to produce small graphs with few components and low max degree, independent of which ring they live on. Distinguishing these requires a deeper sprint, not Bridge A.

**Physics, ontology, or real-world systems.** Nothing about the physical world is in Bridge A's scope.

---

## The Minimal Commitment

Bridge A earns, at most, this sentence:

> "The Path 1 theorem seam on Z/10 and the Path 2 prior-free discovered seams on the tested carriers exhibit shared topological properties — forest-ness, low max degree, small component count — that exceed an edge-count-preserving random-graph baseline by [specific sigma separation]. Whether this resemblance reflects deeper structure or is a consequence of canonical-plus-seam constructions generally producing small sparse graphs is not addressed."

That is the honest ceiling for a PASS outcome. Anything beyond that sentence requires additional sprints.

---

## What a FAIL Would Establish

A FAIL is informative too. It would establish that the topologies of the two seam families do not share measurable structural features beyond density — i.e., that random graphs of matching edge count reproduce the observed pattern well enough that specific structural features cannot be claimed as shared. This would narrow the transport program substantially: it would say that even the most generous structural-overlap question has a negative answer, and any bridge claim between Path 1 and Path 2 objects would need to look elsewhere (maybe Bridge B's overlay-extension angle, maybe a completely different object like the shell partition).

A FAIL does not invalidate Path 1's theorem (unaffected) or Path 2's empirical findings (unaffected). It would only say that Path 1 and Path 2 seams do not share topological family resemblance at the specific features measured.

---

## The Narrow Stance

Bridge A is deliberately narrow. It does not claim to settle big questions. It settles one specific question — do these two classes of seam objects share measurable topology features — and leaves everything else to subsequent sprints. That narrowness is the point. A clean Bridge A result, either way, gives the program a concrete structural fact to build on. A sprawling Bridge A result would give a rich interpretation with weak evidence, which is worse than a tight result either way.
