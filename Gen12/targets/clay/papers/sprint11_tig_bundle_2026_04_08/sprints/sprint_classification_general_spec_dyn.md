## Part 1 — Two Types of Spectral Partition: Exact Definitions

The key structural divide: two qualitatively different classes of "spectral" partition exist for Z/nZ, with distinct sufficiency conditions.

**Definition 1 (Additive Cayley Partition).**
For symmetric S ⊆ Z/nZ (S = −S, 0 ∉ S), define the additive Cayley partition:

π_S^{add}: x ~_S^{add} y  iff  y − x ∈ H = ⟨S⟩₊

where ⟨S⟩₊ is the additive subgroup of (Z/nZ, +) generated by S.

Blocks = cosets of H. For squarefree n with d = gcd(elements of S, n):

**H = ⟨S⟩₊ = d · Z/nZ = {0, d, 2d, ..., n−d}**

**and π_S^{add} = π_d — the partition by residue class mod d.**

*Consequence:* For additive Cayley partitions, the specific shape of S is irrelevant — only d = gcd(S, n) determines the partition. Two sets S₁ and S₂ with the same gcd give the same partition.

U(π_d) = { {x,y} : d | (y−x), x ≠ y }

In CRT coordinates: x ~_{π_d} y iff xᵢ = yᵢ mod pᵢ for every prime pᵢ | d.

---

**Definition 2 (Multiplicative Orbit Partition).**
For symmetric S ⊆ (Z/nZ)* (S = S⁻¹ under multiplication), define the multiplicative orbit partition:

π_S^{mult}: x ~_S^{mult} y  iff  y = s · x for some s ∈ ⟨S⟩ₓ

where ⟨S⟩ₓ is the multiplicative subgroup of (Z/nZ)* generated by S.

Blocks = orbits of ⟨S⟩ₓ acting on Z/nZ by multiplication.

**π_S^{mult} is exactly π_DYN(⟨S⟩ₓ) from prior sprints.**

U(π_S^{mult}) = { {x, y} : y · x⁻¹ ∈ ⟨S⟩ₓ \ {1} for units x } ∪ (non-unit pairs handled by orbit analysis)

---

**Critical distinction:**
- The reflection SPEC from prior sprints (x ~_{SPEC} y iff x + y ≡ 0 mod n, i.e., y = −x) is a **multiplicative orbit** partition: S = {−1}, ⟨S⟩ₓ = {1, −1}.
- The residue partitions π_d (used as "π_{n/2}" in the SPEC+shift theorem) are **additive Cayley** partitions.
- The two types have different sufficiency conditions with DYN — they cannot be reduced to each other.

## Part 2 — Unresolved-Pair Language for Each Type

For the additive type π_d:
U(π_d) ∩ U(π_DYN(g)) ≠ ∅  iff  ∃m ≥ 1 and ∃x ≠ 0 with g^m · x ≠ x: d | (g^m − 1) · x

For the multiplicative type π_S^{mult}:
U(π_S^{mult}) ∩ U(π_DYN(g)) ≠ ∅  iff  ∃c ∈ (⟨S⟩ₓ ∩ ⟨g⟩) with c ≠ 1: taking x=1, the pair {1, c} is a conflict

## Part 3 — Main Theorem A: Multiplicative Spectral + DYN

**Theorem A (Multiplicative SPEC + DYN — Complete).**
For squarefree n, symmetric S ⊆ (Z/nZ)* with S = S⁻¹, and unit g:

{ π_S^{mult}, π_DYN(g) } is sufficient  ⟺  ⟨S⟩ₓ ∩ ⟨g⟩ = {1} in (Z/nZ)*

In coordinatewise form: for every prime pᵢ | n:

**gcd( |⟨S mod pᵢ⟩| , ord_{(Z/pᵢZ)*}(g mod pᵢ) ) = 1**

(where |⟨S mod pᵢ⟩| = order of the subgroup generated by S in (Z/pᵢZ)*)

**Proof.**

(⟸) Suppose ⟨S⟩ₓ ∩ ⟨g⟩ = {1}. If {x,y} ∈ U(π_S^{mult}) ∩ U(π_DYN(g)), then for unit x: y·x⁻¹ ∈ ⟨S⟩ₓ (from π_S class) and y·x⁻¹ ∈ ⟨g⟩ (from DYN orbit). So y·x⁻¹ ∈ ⟨S⟩ₓ ∩ ⟨g⟩ = {1}. Hence y = x, contradiction. □

(⟹) If ⟨S⟩ₓ ∩ ⟨g⟩ ≠ {1}: pick c ≠ 1 in the intersection. The pair {1, c}: c ∈ ⟨S⟩ₓ so {1,c} ∈ U(π_S^{mult}); c ∈ ⟨g⟩ so ∃m: g^m = c, meaning {1,c} ∈ U(π_DYN(g)). Conflict. □

Coordinatewise reduction: In (Z/nZ)* ≅ ∏ᵢ(Z/pᵢZ)*, subgroup intersection is determined componentwise. Each (Z/pᵢZ)* is cyclic, so ⟨A⟩ ∩ ⟨B⟩ has order gcd(|⟨A⟩|, |⟨B⟩|). Intersection = {1} iff gcd = 1 at every component. □

**Special cases of Theorem A:**

| S | ⟨S⟩ₓ | Condition on g |
|---|---|---|
| {−1} | {1,−1} (order 2) | ord_{pᵢ}(g) odd ∀ odd pᵢ | n (← prior sprint theorem) |
| {g₀, g₀⁻¹} | ⟨g₀⟩ | gcd(ord_{pᵢ}(g₀), ord_{pᵢ}(g)) = 1 ∀ pᵢ (← DYN+DYN theorem) |
| (Z/nZ)* | (Z/nZ)* | ⟨g⟩ = {1} only (g = 1, trivial) |
| subgroup G | G | G ∩ ⟨g⟩ = {1} |

**Theorem A is the DYN+DYN theorem applied to G₁ = ⟨S⟩ₓ and G₂ = ⟨g⟩.** The prior sprint's SPEC+DYN theorem is the special case G₁ = {1,−1}.

## Part 4 — Main Theorem B: Additive Cayley + DYN

**Theorem B (Additive Cayley + DYN — Complete).**
For squarefree n = p₁···pₖ, symmetric additive S with d = gcd(S, n), and unit g:

{ π_d^{add}, π_DYN(g) } is sufficient  ⟺  g ≡ 1 mod pⱼ for every prime pⱼ | (n/d)

Equivalently: g acts trivially on all prime components NOT in d, i.e., g is focused on (a subset of) primes dividing d.

**Proof.**

**CRT decomposition of the condition.** In CRT coordinates, x ~_{π_d} y iff xᵢ = yᵢ for every pᵢ | d (the d-primes agree). A pair {x, g^m·x} ∈ U(π_d) ∩ U(π_DYN(g)) requires:
- g^m·x ≠ x (non-trivial orbit move), AND
- xᵢ = (g^m·x)ᵢ = gᵢ^m · xᵢ for every pᵢ | d

The second condition requires: either gᵢ^m = 1 mod pᵢ (no change at pᵢ | d) for all such pᵢ, OR xᵢ = 0 for all pᵢ | d where gᵢ^m ≠ 1.

**(⟹) Necessity.** Suppose g ≢ 1 mod pⱼ for some pⱼ | (n/d). Choose x with xⱼ ≠ 0 and xᵢ = 0 for all i with pᵢ | d. Then for any m ≥ 1 with gⱼ^m ≠ 1: x and g^m·x differ at coordinate j (only). Since all pᵢ | d have xᵢ = 0, the condition "xᵢ = gᵢ^m·xᵢ for pᵢ | d" is trivially satisfied (both sides are 0). So {x, g^m·x} ∈ U(π_d). Since gⱼ ≠ 1 and xⱼ ≠ 0: g^m·xⱼ ≠ xⱼ for m not a multiple of ord_{pⱼ}(g), so g^m·x ≠ x. Conflict. □

**(⟸) Sufficiency.** Suppose g ≡ 1 mod pⱼ for all pⱼ | (n/d). For any x and m ≥ 1: the pair {x, g^m·x}:

At primes pᵢ | d: gᵢ^m · xᵢ may differ from xᵢ (if gᵢ ≠ 1). If it differs: the π_d classes of x and g^m·x are DIFFERENT (they disagree at pᵢ | d). {x, g^m·x} ∉ U(π_d). ✓

At primes pⱼ | (n/d): gⱼ = 1 (by assumption). So gⱼ^m · xⱼ = xⱼ. No change.

If no coordinate changes: g^m·x = x (trivial pair, excluded). If some coordinate changes: it must be at a prime pᵢ | d, which puts x and g^m·x in different π_d classes. No conflict. □

---

**Key structural difference vs. Theorem A:**

Theorem A requires ⟨S⟩ₓ and ⟨g⟩ to be "disjoint" subgroups of (Z/nZ)*.
Theorem B requires g to be "confined" to the primes of d — g must not act outside ⟨S⟩'s domain.

The asymmetry in Theorem B is structural: the additive partition π_d resolves elements only at primes dividing d (ignoring primes of n/d). A DYN action on primes of n/d "escapes" the spectral resolution and always creates conflicts.

## Part 5 — Cross-Type Theorem: Multiplicative Spectral + Additive Partition

**Theorem C (Cross-Type Sufficiency).**
For squarefree n, symmetric multiplicative S ⊆ (Z/nZ)* (S = S⁻¹), and additive Cayley partition π_d^{add}:

{ π_S^{mult}, π_d^{add} } is sufficient  ⟺  the natural map φ: ⟨S⟩ₓ → (Z/dZ)* is injective

Equivalently: no non-identity element of ⟨S⟩ₓ is ≡ 1 mod pᵢ for all pᵢ | d.

**Proof.**

{x, s·x} ∈ U(π_S^{mult}) ∩ U(π_d^{add}) for unit x requires:
- s ∈ ⟨S⟩ₓ \ {1} (spectral pair), AND
- s·x − x = (s−1)·x ∈ d·Z/nZ, i.e., d | (s−1)·x

For unit x with gcd(x,d) = 1: d | (s−1)·x iff d | (s−1) iff s ≡ 1 mod pᵢ for all pᵢ | d.

Conflict exists iff ∃s ≠ 1 in ⟨S⟩ₓ with s ≡ 1 mod pᵢ for all pᵢ | d, i.e., ∃s ≠ 1 in ker(φ).

No conflict iff ker(φ) = {1} iff φ is injective. □

**Verification of prior results under Theorem C:**

*S = {−1} (full reflection SPEC), π_d^{add} (any additive partition):*

φ: {1,−1} → (Z/dZ)* sends −1 to −1 mod d. Injective iff −1 ≢ 1 mod d iff d ∤ 2 iff d has odd prime factor.

For n = 2m (m odd squarefree) and d = m: m is odd, so m ∤ 2. φ injective. {π_SPEC, π_m} sufficient. ✓ (Prior sprint Theorem 2.)

*S = {g₀, g₀⁻¹} (multiplicative pair generating ⟨g₀⟩), π_d^{add}:*

φ: ⟨g₀⟩ → (Z/dZ)*. Injective iff ⟨g₀⟩ ∩ ker(reduction mod d) = {1} iff no power of g₀ is ≡ 1 mod d but ≠ 1 mod n.

## Part 6 — Reduction: Multi-Pair S Adds No New Obstruction Types

**Proposition (Reduction to Single Generator).**
For the multiplicative case: any symmetric S = {a₁, a₁⁻¹, ..., aₖ, aₖ⁻¹} is equivalent to the subgroup G = ⟨a₁,...,aₖ⟩ ≤ (Z/nZ)*. The partition π_S^{mult} = π_DYN(G). The sufficiency condition {π_S^{mult}, π_DYN(g)} reduces to G ∩ ⟨g⟩ = {1}.

For the additive case: any symmetric S with gcd(S,n) = d gives π_S^{add} = π_d. The sufficiency condition depends only on d.

**Corollary.** Multi-pair symmetric sets create NO new obstruction type beyond single-generator cases. The obstruction is determined entirely by ⟨S⟩ (multiplicative) or gcd(S,n) (additive).

**Why no new obstructions arise:**

For multiplicative S: U(π_S^{mult}) is the edge set of the Cayley graph of ⟨S⟩ acting on Z/nZ. Adding more generators to S can only ENLARGE ⟨S⟩, making the partition COARSER and the sufficiency condition HARDER to satisfy. The condition ⟨S⟩ ∩ ⟨g⟩ = {1} captures the full structure — no "spectral blur" from multiple generators creates an obstruction not already present from the subgroup ⟨S⟩.

For additive S: U(π_S^{add}) depends only on d = gcd(S,n). Adding more symmetric pairs either leaves d unchanged (no effect on the partition) or decreases d (coarsens the partition, making sufficiency harder). The condition is always "g trivial outside primes of d."

## Part 7 — Examples: n = 30, 42, 66, 70

## Part 9 — Outcome Classification

**Outcome A (Exact criterion in subgroup language): TRUE for all three cases.**

- Multiplicative + DYN: G ∩ ⟨g⟩ = {1} in (Z/nZ)*.
- Additive + DYN: g trivial on primes of n/d.
- Cross-type: injectivity of G → (Z/dZ)*.

All three are exact, clean, group-theoretic conditions.

**Outcome B (Only bounds, not exact): FALSE.** The conditions are exact, not merely bounding.

**Outcome C (Wild behavior beyond single-pair): FALSE.**

Multi-pair S creates no new obstruction. The subgroup ⟨S⟩ fully captures the structure; adding more generators either increases ⟨S⟩ (harder to be disjoint from ⟨g⟩) or leaves ⟨S⟩ unchanged (same condition). The single-pair theorem is the canonical base case, and the general theorem is its subgroup-theoretic generalization.

## Part 10 — Reduction of General SPEC to Single-Generator

**Theorem D (Reduction).**
For the multiplicative case: {π_S^{mult}, π_DYN(g)} with S = {s₁, s₁⁻¹, ..., sₖ, sₖ⁻¹} is equivalent to {π_DYN(G), π_DYN(g)} where G = ⟨s₁,...,sₖ⟩. The sufficiency condition is G ∩ ⟨g⟩ = {1}.

**Coordinatewise:** At each prime pᵢ, the condition gcd(|G mod pᵢ|, ord_{pᵢ}(g)) = 1 replaces the single-pair condition gcd(ord_{pᵢ}(s₁), ord_{pᵢ}(g)) = 1. The multi-pair case uses |G mod pᵢ| = lcm of orders of all generators at pᵢ (in the cyclic group (Z/pᵢZ)*). No genuinely new structure.

**For additive case:** {π_S^{add}, π_DYN(g)} = {π_d, π_DYN(g)} where d = gcd(S,n). The specific generators of S are completely irrelevant — only d matters. No new structure.

## Summary

**Theorem A (proved):** {π_S^{mult}, π_DYN(g)} sufficient iff ⟨S⟩ₓ ∩ ⟨g⟩ = {1} in (Z/nZ)*. Coordinatewise: gcd(|⟨S mod pᵢ⟩|, ord_{pᵢ}(g)) = 1 for all pᵢ | n. This is the DYN+DYN theorem with G₁ = ⟨S⟩ₓ.

**Theorem B (proved):** {π_d^{add}, π_DYN(g)} sufficient iff g ≡ 1 mod pⱼ for every prime pⱼ | (n/d). This is structurally different from Theorem A — an "orientation" condition, not a disjointness condition.

**Theorem C (proved):** {π_S^{mult}, π_d^{add}} sufficient iff the map ⟨S⟩ₓ → (Z/dZ)* is injective. This unifies the prior sprint's SPEC+shift theorem (S={-1}, d=m odd: injectivity of {1,-1}→(Z/mZ)* holds iff −1 ≢ 1 mod m, which holds for m odd ≥ 3).

**Theorem D (proved):** Multi-pair S reduces entirely to ⟨S⟩. No new obstruction types emerge beyond single-generator analysis.

---

**Strongest honest claim:**
> The complete classification of sufficient {π_S, π_DYN(g)} pairs splits along two structural lines: multiplicative spectral vs. additive Cayley. For multiplicative S: the theorem is the DYN+DYN subgroup-intersection condition G ∩ ⟨g⟩ = {1}, with the REFL SPEC being the special case G = {1,−1}. For additive S (CRT residue structure): the theorem requires g to be trivial outside the primes of d — an orientation condition not present in the multiplicative case. Multi-pair S adds no new obstruction types in either case.

**Strongest honest boundary:**
> These three theorems (A, B, C) classify all pairs where one partition comes from a recognized algebraic family (multiplicative orbit, additive CRT, or their cross). What remains unclassified: (1) pairs where S has no clean algebraic generating structure (non-group-generating S), (2) the full classification of cross-type {π_G^{mult}, π_d^{add}} beyond the REFL case and single-pair — specifically, which pairs (G,d) satisfy the injectivity condition and how this interacts with the prime structure of n, (3) extensions beyond squarefree n.

**Open problem:**
> The three theorems use qualitatively different conditions (subgroup intersection, orientation, injectivity). Can they be unified under a single categorical framework? Conjecture: yes, via the notion of "orthogonal" partitions in a more general lattice-theoretic sense, where the three conditions correspond to three ways that two partition-generating objects can fail to interact.
