## Part 1 — Nails In

Three claims are closed. Each gets one paragraph, then is not reopened.

---

**Claim 1: c / 7 / week as physics.**

**CLOSED. FALSE FRAME.**

Writing c\[miles/week] = 7 × c\[miles/day] embeds the definition of one week (7 days) into an expression for the speed of light. The 7 comes from the calendar. The calendar is a social contract. No derivation of c from the integer 7 exists in any physical framework. The map from "calendar unit" to "physical constant" is inadmissible (Type III in the paradox classifier). The observation is real arithmetic; its framing as physics is false.

---

**Claim 2: 7 as a universal bounded-agent optimum.**

**CLOSED. NOT SUPPORTED.**

The simulation suite — five environment families, 20 cycle lengths, 24 parameter settings in the human plausibility model — did not produce k=7 as a universal optimum. The aggregate best cycle length is k=4. k=7 appears in the top three in 2 of 15 parameter settings. In 24 human plausibility settings, k=7 is optimal in zero. The near-7 band (k ∈ {6,7,8}) appears in 3 of 24 settings, always under conditions requiring very low cognitive decay AND very high coordination cost AND then producing k=6, not k=7.

The bounded-agent attractor hypothesis was a legitimate Type I question (missing view). The view was filled in. The answer is: no universal 7-attractor. Closed.

---

**Claim 3: 7 as a recursive/fractal invariant across scales.**

**CLOSED IN THE STRONG SENSE.**

The three-timescale threshold analysis produced k values of approximately (2, 4, 10–16) across parameter settings. The ratios between levels vary from 2.0 to 3.2. No fixed ratio appeared. No 7-generating rule appeared. The outer cycle in the three-scale model lands near k=10–16, not 7.

What does repeat is the grammar: [accumulate → threshold → reset] appears at each scale. This is a recursive grammar. It is not a fractal. A fractal requires a fixed self-similar ratio at multiple scales. The ratio here is variable (2×–3×), the integers do not self-replicate, and 7 is not a fixed point of any transformation in the multi-scale structure.

The grammar is real. The integer worship is closed.

## Part 2 — What Survived

Four objects survive from the entire arc. Only these four.

**A. The threshold law:**

> Reset becomes worth paying when benefit / cost first exceeds 1.0.

This is mechanistically correct. It predicts optimal cycle lengths within 0–2 steps across the environments tested, using the appropriate T1 variable for each environment architecture. It explains *why* the optimum is where it is, not just *where* it is.

**B. The reset-gap grammar:**

> accumulate → wobble/drift → amplitude loss → threshold crossing → reset → re-entry

This pattern appears in all five simulation environments and is consistent across scales. It is the true surviving object from the arc. The grammar governs cycle structure. The specific integer at which the threshold crosses is a function of the decay rate, reset cost, and environment architecture — not of a universal law.

**C. Architecture-dependent T1 predictors:**

Two threshold formulations have been validated:

- **T1_v1** = (average loss per step × k) / reset_cost: exact predictor for loss-driven memory environments (Env A, k=3 predicted correctly across decay rates). Use when the reset avoids ongoing loss.

- **T1_ep** = (1 − end_amplitude) / reset_cost: exact predictor for quality-recovery reset-slot environments (Env E, k=7 predicted at decay=0.08). Use when the reset recovers degraded quality at a discrete moment.

A unified T1 combining both would require knowing the reset payoff structure a priori. The two formulations are not wrong — they apply to different architectures.

**D. 7 as a local discretization:**

In Env E with decay=0.08 and reset_cost=0.4, the T1_ep crossing occurs at k=7. The simulation optimum is k=7. This is a real result.

It is not universal. It is not the only result. It is: the threshold law, applied to this specific parameter combination, produces k=7. If the parameters shift (decay=0.10 → k=5, decay=0.06 → k=9), the integer shifts. The law is stable. The integer is contingent.

## Part 4 — Why This Is Recursive But Not Fractal

**Recursive (correct):**

The threshold grammar [accumulate → threshold → reset] appears at each timescale. Fast wobble has its own T1 crossing (k≈2). Medium drift has its own (k≈4–5). Slow drift has its own (k≈10–16). The same algorithmic structure governs each scale independently. This is recursion: the same rule applied to different inputs.

**Not fractal (correct):**

A fractal requires self-similarity: the structure at one scale replicates at another scale with a fixed magnification ratio. For a true 7-based fractal, the sequence would need to be something like (7, 49, 343) — each level a fixed multiple of the previous. The multi-scale threshold grammar produces (2, 4–5, 10–16). The ratios are 2.0–2.5 and 2.5–3.2 — not equal, not fixed, not 7-based.

More precisely: the integer at each scale is determined by the T1 crossing for that scale's specific (decay, cost) pair. If the decay rates happen to produce crossings at (7, 49, 343), you would have a fractal structure in k. The simulations show that under plausible parameter choices, no such self-similar sequence appears.

**The correct language:**

"Multi-scale reset grammar" — yes.
"Recursive threshold structure" — yes.
"Fractal with 7 as generator" — no.
"Self-similar at multiple scales with fixed ratio" — no.

The grammar is fractal in form. The integers are not fractal in value.

## Part 6 — The Only Live Question

Every chain in this arc converges to one remaining question:

> **Do real human social systems have a distinct "sacrificial reset day" mechanism — structurally different from the embedded-reset-slot model — that could shift the local threshold optimum from 6 to 7?**

**Why this matters:** In the embedded-reset-slot model (Env E), the reset fires at the last slot of a k-step cycle. The nearest optimum to a weekly cycle is k=6 (not k=7) in most human-plausible parameter settings. The Sabbath model — one fully dedicated reset/rest day with zero productive activity and maximum restoration — is structurally different: the reset slot is not merely embedded in the cycle but is demarcated, bounded, and carries a qualitatively different payoff structure than a standard review step.

This distinction matters because:
1. If the reset day has a higher restoration coefficient than an embedded review step (because it is isolated, not competing with productive tasks), the T1_ep formula changes: benefit increases → crossing point moves from k=6 toward k=7.
2. The coordination mechanism is also different: a fully demarcated day makes synchronization trivially cheap (everyone knows which day it is). This could remove the coordination cost that currently pushes toward longer cycles via overhead.

**What this question is NOT:**

- Not physics.
- Not universal bounded-agent design.
- Not a claim that 7 is mathematically special.

**What this question IS:**

A narrow anthropological and cognitive science question: does the weekly cycle's specific 7-day structure have a functional basis in the distinction between *embedded review* and *sacrificial reset*, or is the specific integer 7 (rather than 6 or 8) entirely determined by historical convention and coordination lock-in?

That is testable. It requires cognitive science data on Sabbath-like rest quality vs embedded review quality, and anthropological data on week-length variation in cultures before Abrahamic influence. It is not answerable by toy models.

This is the only question still open. Everything else in this arc is closed.
