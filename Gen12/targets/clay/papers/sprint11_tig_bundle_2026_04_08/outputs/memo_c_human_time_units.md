## Layer 1 — Exact Unit Conversion

**Speed of light (exact, by SI definition since 1983):**

c = 299,792,458 m/s

**Exact conversion factors:**

- 1 international mile = 1,609.344 m (exact, by international agreement)
- 1 day = 86,400 s (exact)
- 1 week = 604,800 s (exact: 7 × 86,400)

**c in miles per second:**

c = 299,792,458 / 1,609.344 = **186,282.3971...** miles/s

**c in miles per day:**

c = 186,282.3971... × 86,400 = **16,094,799,109** miles/day (≈ 1.609 × 10¹⁰)

**c in miles per week:**

c = 16,094,799,109 × 7 = **112,663,593,766** miles/week (≈ 1.127 × 10¹¹)

**Audit of "c = 7 × …" phrasing:**

The relationship c\[miles/week] = 7 × c\[miles/day] is dimensionally exact and numerically correct. The factor 7 is **not** a property of c — it is the definition of 1 week = 7 days. Writing this as "c = 7 × (c in miles/day)" is correct shorthand for a unit conversion, not a statement that 7 appears in the physics of light.

There is no sense in which c "contains" a factor of 7 in natural units, SI units, Planck units, or any unit system whose base units are defined by physical constants rather than calendrical conventions.

## Layer 2 — Human-Time Grammar

The number 7 appears in this expression for one reason: the 7-day week.

**What the week is:**
- The 7-day week is a human cultural and religious convention attested across ancient Babylonian, Hebrew, Roman, and other traditions.
- It has no known derivation from a physical constant, orbital period, or measurable quantity.
- The closest astronomical candidate — the lunar month — is approximately 29.5 days, not evenly divisible by 7.

**What this means for the formula:**

When we write c\[miles/week] = 7 × c\[miles/day], the 7 is functioning as a **reporting-unit multiplier**, not as a structural feature of electromagnetism. If human civilization had adopted 5-day or 10-day weeks, we would write c\[miles/5-day-week] = 5 × c\[miles/day] and observe that "5 appears in the expression for c."

The formula is numerically real. The temptation to read it as structurally significant is a units-grammar artifact.
