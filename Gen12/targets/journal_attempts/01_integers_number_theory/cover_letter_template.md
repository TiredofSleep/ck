Dear Editor,

I submit for your consideration *The sinc$^{2}$ Zero Law in Prime Arithmetic*, a short note on an exact zero-set characterization of the squared sinc function on rational arguments with prime denominator.

The main result is stated cleanly: for any prime $p$ and integer $k$, $\sinc^{2}(k/p)=0$ if and only if $p\mid k$. Inside the corridor $k\in\{1,\ldots,p\}$ the unique zero is at the endpoint $k=p$, and every interior position is provably nonzero as a consequence of primality, not of numerical observation. The paper extracts three immediate corollaries — loop closure, fold necessity, and no-shortcut — and records the exact boundary value $\sinc^{2}(1/2)=4/\pi^{2}$ together with its cosine identity. A one-paragraph Fourier remark connects the result to Montgomery's pair-correlation framework at the level of basis functions. A companion script, `proof_d25_loop_closure.py`, verifies the zero-set and boundary identities across all primes $p\le 199$ with zero exceptions.

The note is self-contained, finite, and runnable; it does not depend on any private framework and the proof is two lines. I think it fits the Integers scope well: a small exact statement about primes and corridors, with an electronic supplement that executes in seconds. I also send it in the hope that it will be useful as a teaching exercise — the result is a clean one-page exhibit of "primality forces the zero set" and students can verify the script themselves.

Supplementary materials: `proof_d25_loop_closure.py`; the same text is available at DOI 10.5281/zenodo.18852047. The manuscript contains no funded conflicts. I certify that the work is original and not under consideration elsewhere.

Thank you for your time.
