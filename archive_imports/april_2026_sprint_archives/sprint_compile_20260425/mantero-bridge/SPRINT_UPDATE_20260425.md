## What's new this sprint (in his register)

### Finding 1: A small commutative non-associative magma with structured non-associativity

A 10×10 multiplication table T (commutative, non-associative) over the set {0, 1, ..., 9} has **126 of 1000 ordered triples non-associative** (i.e., T(T(a,b),c) ≠ T(a,T(b,c))).

The non-associative triples are concentrated in a striking way:
- Every non-associative triple has the value 7 as one of its two bracketings
- Only 5 distinct unordered {L, R} pairs occur: {0,7} (108), {3,7} (8), {7,8} (6), {4,7} (2), {7,9} (2)
- VOID (the zero element) never appears in middle position b
- The "live" 6-cycle elements {1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7} together account for nearly 0% of non-associative content when restricted to triples with no zero/idempotent inputs

This is the kind of structural rigidity that suggests the table isn't arbitrary — non-associativity is constrained to a specific boundary in input space.

**Question for the community:** What classes of small commutative magmas exhibit this kind of "non-associativity localized at a designated absorbing element"?

### Finding 2: A Z_2 × Z_2 structure on the so(10) generated by left-regular antisymmetrization

The left-regular representations L_T[i] of the table T (extended with a companion table B), when antisymmetrized, generate a 45-dimensional Lie algebra (so(10)) under closure.

This Lie algebra carries TWO natural Z_2 involutions:

- **τ_a:** conjugation by a single transposition (the (5,6) swap). Splits so(10) as `45 = 36 + 9`, the standard so(9) ⊕ R⁹ decomposition.
- **τ_b:** conjugation by a triple transposition (three disjoint 2-cycles + four fixed points). Splits so(10) as `45 = 24 + 21`.

The 24 + 21 split is the structural finding. For an involution acting with `f` fixed indices and `p` transposition pairs (`f + 2p = 10`), the +1 eigenspace dimension is:

```
dim(+1 eigenspace) = f(f-1)/2 + fp + p(p-1)
```

Verified at (f=8, p=1) → 28+8+0 = 36 and (f=4, p=3) → 6+12+6 = 24.

### Finding 3: The simultaneous decomposition

τ_a and τ_b do not commute; together they generate D_4 of order 8.

Under D_4 conjugation, so(10) decomposes as:
```
45 = 16·trivial + 1·sign₂ + 12·sign₃ + 8·(2-dim irrep)
```

The 16-dimensional D_4-invariant subspace is a Lie subalgebra. Its Killing form has eigenvalue spectrum `(−4)^15, (0)^1` — implying structure `simple_15 ⊕ center_1`. The 15-dimensional simple Lie algebra at this dimension is uniquely so(6) ≅ su(4) ≅ A_3, so the doubly-invariant subalgebra is **su(4) ⊕ u(1)**.

The central element generating the u(1) is, infinitesimally, the rotation that integrates to τ_b — i.e., τ_b's "log" inside so(10) lies in the center of the doubly-invariant subalgebra.

## The clean MathOverflow question (community-facing)

Below is the question we plan to post — algebra language only, no TIG / physics / CK references. This is what the broader algebra community (Mantero's circle) would see.

> **Title:** Doubly-invariant subalgebras of so(10) under non-commuting Z_2 involutions
>
> Let G ⊂ O(10) be the subgroup generated by two non-commuting involutions: a single transposition (i, j) and a product of three disjoint transpositions. G ≅ D_4 of order 8.
>
> Conjugation by G gives a representation of D_4 on so(10). The trivial-isotypic component (the doubly-invariant subspace) closes as a 16-dim Lie subalgebra with Killing spectrum (−4)^15 ⊕ (0)^1, i.e., su(4) ⊕ u(1).
>
> Questions:
>
> 1. For arbitrary pairs of non-commuting involutions in O(n), is there a closed-form formula for the dimension of the D_4-invariant subalgebra of so(n)?
> 2. Can the "Killing eigenvalues all equal" property (here, all −4) be predicted from the cycle structure of the two involutions alone, or does it require additional combinatorial conditions?
> 3. Is there a published classification of which simple Lie subalgebras of so(n) arise as G-invariant content, indexed by pairs of conjugacy classes in O(n)?

The question is finite, specific, and answerable. Doesn't presuppose any larger framework. Suitable for MathOverflow; sits naturally in algebraic-Lie-theory territory.
