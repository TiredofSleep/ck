## What we do for you

When you bring a framework to us, you can expect the following moves applied to your work. Each one is portable and each one has been road-tested on our own sixteen-sprint arc.

**Separate what's packed.** Most frameworks arrive with three claims inside one sentence. We pull them apart into separate statements, each with its own scope and its own rubric. If your "the framework is confirmed" is really four distinct verdicts, we will help you write it as four sentences.

**Name the gap rather than paper it.** If your test returns an ambiguous result, the move is not to rerun under a slightly relaxed rubric. The move is to articulate what specifically was ambiguous, locate the hinge in your framework's vocabulary, and file the gap as a named open question rather than dissolving it into noise.

**File at the right register.** Not every useful claim is a theorem. Most are hypotheses, structural observations, or conjectures. Each register has its own discipline, and conflating them is the single most common way frameworks lose credibility with outside reviewers. We help you find the register that preserves your claim without inflating it — usually anchored to **one sanctioned sentence** that you can defend in front of a reviewer and that paraphrases of are explicitly out of bounds.

**Keep threads separate.** If your framework mixes physics, combinatorics, and a personal philosophy in the same paragraph, we will not help you build a bridge. We will help you pull the three apart, label each, and keep them distinct until a proved map between them shows up. "Suggestive, not bridged" is a reusable annotation for patterns you notice across threads.

**Preserve your earlier work.** Your older attempts are not trash. They are historical register. We install a `[HISTORICAL]` header discipline and an archive-branch pattern that lets you mark superseded material in place without losing it. Never-delete is a hard policy on our side and we recommend it on yours.

**Pre-register the rubric.** If you are about to run a test, the rubric should be written *before* the result arrives, even if the rubric is rough. Retrofitted rubrics are not rubrics and reviewers can smell them.

**Refuse composite claims.** If verdict A is PASS and verdict B is PASS, that does not license "the framework is confirmed." Each verdict stays a separate sentence. This is our Rule 19 and it is load-bearing across the whole arc.

## How to bring your framework

We can ingest large files and framework documents. Tangled is fine — tangled is actually *better* than pre-polished, because a polished pitch has usually already hidden the seams we most need to see.

**What to bring:**

- Your framework document(s), in whatever form they currently exist — markdown, PDF, plain text, Jupyter notebooks, code, LaTeX, scanned handwritten notes if that is what you have.
- Your earlier attempts, especially the ones you now think were wrong. Do not clean these out.
- Any tests, proofs, or computational results you have run, even if the results were unclear.
- Your own attempt at a map of the whole thing, however rough.
- A short note — one page is plenty — describing what you think is cashed out, what you think is structural, and what you think is conjectural. You will probably revise all three categories during the resolution pass. That is expected.

**What NOT to bring:**

- A finished elevator pitch. If it already reads like a journal abstract, send the drafts that came before it instead.
- Pre-merged composite claims. Send the component statements separately if you have them.
- Someone else's framework you are trying to import wholesale. The resolution methodology works on *your* frame; imported frameworks need their own independent resolution from their own author.

**Channels:**

- **GitHub issues:** open an issue at `github.com/TiredofSleep/ck` with the label `collaboration-intake`. Attach or link your documents. This is the preferred channel because it creates a public-if-you-want record and lets us cross-reference the methodology as we work.
- **Email:** `brayden.ozark@gmail.com`. Use this for material you are not ready to share publicly, or if you would rather start the conversation directly with Brayden before opening a public issue. Private-until-you-choose is respected.
- **Direct via CK:** for short questions or single-claim checks, speak to him at coherencekeeper.com. For full framework submission, use the two channels above.

## What happens next

1. **Intake.** We receive your material. No immediate response is expected to be a verdict — we read first.
2. **Separation pass.** We pull your tangled claims into distinct statements and return a draft map, clearly marked as *our* read of your work, subject to your correction. You keep editorial authority over what your framework claims.
3. **Register filing.** Each statement gets filed as proved / structural / conjectural / hypothesis. Each hypothesis gets a sanctioned sentence.
4. **Gap naming.** Unresolved places get articulated as named open questions rather than glossed.
5. **Deliverable.** A short resolution document — typically 3 to 8 pages — that you can use with reviewers, funders, collaborators, or just for your own clarity. You own the document; we retain methodology credit where our patterns (IG1–IG5, sanctioned-sentence discipline, three-threads-separate, etc.) appear.
6. **Iteration.** Frameworks rarely resolve in one pass. Expect two to four rounds, at the pace of research, not the pace of consulting.

## The disciplines we hold

These are non-negotiable on our side and we ask collaborators to respect them in material we work on together.

- **Never delete.** Superseded material is marked `[HISTORICAL]` in place and preserved in an archive branch or folder. Your early drafts are part of the record.
- **Cite everything.** Every term with external precedent gets cited. Novel terms get flagged as `[NOVEL — extends X]`.
- **Three threads separate.** Distinct mathematical or conceptual tracks stay distinct until a proved map shows up. No vocabulary import across tracks without justification.
- **No composite claims.** Each verdict is its own sentence. Multiple PASS verdicts do not sum to a stronger claim. (Rule 19.)
- **Sanctioned-sentence discipline for hypotheses.** Each hypothesis at foundation register gets exactly one sentence that is allowed to be used. Paraphrases that strengthen the connection ("supports," "suggests," "points toward") are explicitly out of bounds.
- **Pre-register rubrics.** Write the test criteria before the test.
- **Honest UNCLEAR verdicts.** If the rubric returns ambiguity, report ambiguity. Do not rerun until you get the answer you wanted.
- **Don't ventriloquize CK.** Never write prose in CK's voice. Let his architecture find his words. Arithmetic and direct verification are the only exceptions.

## What we don't do

- **We don't prove your theorem for you.** We help you see clearly whether you have a theorem, a hypothesis, or a structural observation — and help you write each at the right register.
- **We don't stitch threads together.** If your physics and your philosophy are suggesting the same pattern, that is an observation worth recording; it is not a proof of shared mechanism.
- **We don't promote hypotheses to theorems.** Ever. Not for grant applications, not for journal submissions, not for PR.
- **We don't ghostwrite.** Your voice stays yours. Our role is resolution, not substitution.
- **We don't flatten mystery.** If something in your framework is genuinely unresolved and genuinely generative, we help you hold it open rather than forcing premature closure.
- **We don't guarantee verdicts.** A resolution pass may conclude that your framework is not yet ready to claim what you hoped it would claim. That is itself a useful result.
